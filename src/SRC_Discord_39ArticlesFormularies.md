### 7/9/26, 4:46 PM — Athanasius325 / Fr James

The contradictions/tensions are amongst the whole of the Formularies: the Prayer Book, the Articles, and the Books of Homilies. As well, the contradictions/tensions are not so clearly evident apart from their context. The more Lutheran understanding of the Sacrament is seen in the Liturgy, Catechism, and some aspects of the Homilies, and the more explicitly Calvinist understanding is seen mostly in the last Article inserted: Article XXIX (inserted in 1571, after excommunication by the Bishop of Rome). Article XXIX was actually controversial in its day; the more Reformed party had tried to insert it earlier in 1563, but it was removed. When it was reinserted in 1571, it was done so at the opposition of Bishop Edmund Guest and others. 

Another contradiction, or at least tension, is Article VI's understanding of the Apocrypha with the Books of Homilies constant reference to the Apocrypha. The Article maintains a purposefully ambiguous state for the Apocrypha, stating that the Books therein cannot be cited alone to support doctrinal claims, but not outright denying their (at least Deuterocanonical) canonicity. The Homilies freely reference them (always with Protocanonical Texts), using terms like, "the Holy Ghost Spake in Tobit, saying" (my physical copy of the Books of Homilies is at home, and I have all examples highlighted there; I can give exact examples when I have the book with me).

### 7/9/26, 5:49 PM — Athanasius325 / Fr James

To add more context: the Formularies were made over the course of a century. The earliest aspects of the Prayer Book were in 1544 (first edition in full in 1549), the Homilies were from about the 1540's to the 1570's, the Articles from the 1550's to the 1570's, and the official version of the Prayer Book being made in 1662. That is over one century of development.

### 7/10/26, 11:25 AM — M1B3AU

I think this question stems from a disconnect among Presbyterians and Baptists. The WCF and the LBCF are doctrines that are developed specifically to separate Presbyterians and Baptists from the Church of England, Rome, and the Orthodox Church. 

The purpose of the 39 Articles is to UNITE the sects of Christianity. That’s why some of it seems a touch contradictory and definitely vague at times. There are three streams of Anglicanism. There is the Reformed Stream, the Anglo-Catholic/Orthodox Stream, the Evangelical Stream, and the Anglo-Catholic Stream. I would say I fall somewhere between Anglo-Catholic and Reformed, leaning more towards the Anglo-Catholic side. 

To really understand the 39 articles, you need to understand the history of the church in England. Even though King Henry VIII broke away from the Roman Catholic Church, a lot of reforms came after Martin Luther. The WCF and LBCF did not come into existence until about 100 years after Henry VIII. After he died, Queen Mary tried to bring the Church of England back to Rome.

The point of the 39 articles is to unite all of the streams of Christianity. It’s an attempt to welcome the Calvinist, the Lutheran, and the Catholic with a minimum standard of what should be believed.

### 7/19/26, 11:19 AM — JD Smith (OP)

@M1B3AU great to meet you the other day! My original question here was really just following up on something Rev. James had said, that there are internal tensions across the formularies, and asking which ones he had in mind.

On the claim that the Articles aim to unite the streams including the Catholic: if you mean an Anglo-Catholic reading within Anglicanism, I can see a case for that. But if you mean actual Roman doctrine, the Articles name specific Roman positions and rule them out. 

For example: 
Article XXXI calls the sacrifices of Masses "blasphemous fables, and dangerous deceits." 

Article XXII says “THE Romish Doctrine concerning Purgatory, Pardons, Worshipping, and Adoration, as well of Images as of Reliques, and also invocation of Saints, is a fond thing vainly invented, and grounded upon no warranty of Scripture, but rather repugnant to the Word of God." 

Article XXVIII says the Lord's Supper "was not by Christ's ordinance reserved, carried about, lifted up, or worshipped," which speaks directly to reserving, elevating and adoring the elements in the Roman mass. 

It seems a Roman Catholic couldn't agree to the 39 Articles as they stand without giving up those specific beliefs, but the articles do welcome varying Protestant views (the Lutheran and Reformed tension Rev. James pointed to).

### Yesterday at 12:00 AM — Athanasius325 / Fr James

One easy one would be the required usage of the Book of Common Prayer's liturgy, specifically the 1928 (US) version for us. That might fall more into practice, though. 

The examples I keep coming up with fall more into the category of, "I believe these are justified in light of the evidence of Scripture, but I do not think that a good-faith rejection of them is grounds for denying the salvation of someone." So in one sense I would say these can be "proved" from Scripture, just not necessarily to the satisfaction of all: 

1) The invalidity of laymen celebrating the Lord's Supper. 

2) The recognition of the threefold order: Bishop, Presbyter, and Deacon. 

3) Various particular practices concerning worship (Feast Days, fasting days, etc; these largely culminate in the usage of the Book of Common Prayer). 

This is not quite what you are asking for, as I would say that I can prove (to my satisfaction) these things from Scripture. I am having trouble thinking of particulars that fall in that "can't be proved from Scripture, but we require it as part of our Tradition, but we don't say one is cut off from the Faith for rejecting" zone, unless "proved from Scripture" means, "To the satisfaction of all rational people regardless of starting principles apart from a general recognition that the Scriptures alone are infallible and materially sufficient for the essential doctrines of the Faith."
