### 7/9/26, 5:20 PM — Athanasius325 / Fr James

There is a wide range of understandings of the Regulative Principle. The most extreme I can think of is: exclusive Psalms, required a capella, No images, crosses, etc. No worship except on Sundays. No observance of various Feast Days, even Christmas and Easter. In fact, the elders actively avoid topics like the Resurrection during Easter and the Birth of our Lord near Christmas (again, this is the most extreme example). No recitation of Creeds (Apostles', Nicene, Athanasian). 

I would say that the Biblical witness does not allow for these as commands. 

1) Psalms are not exclusively required. Matthew 26:30 shows our Lord and the Apostles singing an hymn together (ὑμνήσαντες; the word for "Psalms" is ψαλμοῖς). 

2) A capella is not required. Psalm 150 makes little sense in light of the fact that it lists all sorts of instruments for praise. The counter to this is that praise is not the same as worship. The problem is that praise is an element of worship, and the Psalm (and the rest of Scripture) does not say, "Praise Him with these instruments, unless it's in the form of worship." 

3) Images are all throughout the worship of the Old Testament, and even commanded by God, from the bronze serpent (Numbers 21:4–9) to the Temple itself (1 Kings 6; 2 Chronicles 3),  to the Ark of the Covenant. The Ark had two Cherubim on it (Exodus 25:18-22), and Joshua and the Elders of Israel rightly bowed down before the Ark in Joshua 7:6. 1 Chronicles 28:2 identifies the Ark as "the footstool of our God". The Psalms speak of bowing down before that footstool (Psalm 99:5, 132:7-8). Moses meets God Face-to-face there (Exodus 25:22). We are not to bow to the images in the sense of obeisance to them, but to the untrained eye seeing Joshua and the Elders bow before the Presence of the Lord at the Ark would look like bowing to the images of the Cherubim. 

(continued)

### 7/9/26, 5:43 PM — Athanasius325 / Fr James

4) The command to only worship on Sundays is inconsistent with the Biblical witness. First, the witness of Acts shows daily gathering for worship (Acts 2:46–47). Second, interestingly, the Apostles also worshipped at the Temple (Acts 3:1), and at the synagogues (Acts 13:14–15; 17:1–3; these are obviously for evangelism, too). Third, Christ Himself participated in "extrabiblical" Feast Days: namely, He participated in the Feast of Dedication, Hannukah, which comes from 1 Maccabees 4:52–59 (this is found in John 10:22–23). So according to the Biblical witness, Feast Days can be added to the Calendar apart from Divine Command (given that those who hold to this understanding of the RPW would uniformly reject 1 Maccabees as Scripture). 

5) Concerning no recitation of the Creeds, this gets into a really odd territory. If the claim is that the Creeds are, in substance, opposed to Scripture, that would be one thing. I don't know any RPW people who would claim that. To oppose the recitation of the Creeds based upon the fact that the exact word-order is not directly found in Scripture would be like opposing a homily or a prayer because the exact word-order is not directly found in Scripture. This would leave us with a handful of homilies (few are recorded in Scripture), and for corporate prayers just the Psalms, the Lord's Prayer, and other prayers like the Nunc Dimittis, Magnificat, and Benedictus (the last three, ironically, opposed by the early Puritans as Canticles for not being Psalms; see their petition to King James VI and I) available. It also seems to be an assertion that the Regulative Principle does not apply to personal worship, so arguably one could not even personally pray apart from the following of these commands.

### 7/11/26, 8:43 AM — Athanasius325 / Fr James

One hypothetical I can give is this: in the early Church some Christians would give milk and honey to the newly Baptized. Suppose this became a universal tradition: I do not believe it would violate a proper understanding of the RPW, because the RPW is about principles and not individual acts. I think the biggest issue I have with how the RPW works for many today is how it's used for individual practices, demanding those EXACT practices be found in Scripture, rather than principles that are drawn from the witness of Scripture. 

So a principle I would be using to justify this example is this: that imagery and symbolism are to reflect the Biblical witness. The milk and honey very clearly point to the Promised Land, which ties well with both Baptism and the Old Testament. Where others holding to the RPW will point to the necessity of finding particular acts, I am looking for particular principles. They would say, "We see no example of milk and honey being given after Baptism, therefore we must reject that." I would say, "We see examples of imagery being tied to their fulfillment, and this act ties the imagery of the Promised Land to Baptism, so this fits that principle well." 

If, instead of the milk and honey, the Baptizer did something like quote 1 Corinthians 10:1-2 either before or after the Baptism, I think all but the most stringent of RPW people would have no issue with it. But the individual act of quoting that exact Passage before/after Baptism is not in Scripture. It makes no sense to me to prohibit acts like the drinking of milk and honey but to not prohibit the act of quoting Scripture. The act of drinking milk and honey is, I humbly submit, very clearly an act of pointing to Scripture.

### Yesterday at 11:42 AM — Athanasius325 / Fr James

It is the first. If there was an example of prayers to the Saints in Heaven in the Scriptures, an example that was lauded or not treated as sinful, then we would of necessity have to find it appropriate. 

The phrase in Revelation 5:8 translated as, "prayers of the saints" is, "προσευχαὶ τῶν ἁγίων". The word "τῶν" is translated as "of the" (it is in the genitive), and cannot be rightly translated as "to the" (that would be "τοῖς"). If St John had written "to the" instead of "of the", then this would be a good, strong Passage in defense of prayers to the Saints in Heaven. As it stands, what I see from these Passages are: 

1) Saints pray (Saints are not limited to Heaven; St Paul calls people on earth Saints as well). 

2) Their prayers are heard and offered in the Heavenly worship. 

The closest we can get to prayers to the Saints is this: The Saints in Heaven pray, and those prayers are offered in Heaven. While I wholeheartedly agree that the Saints in Heaven are praying for us, I do not see how that necessarily leads to the claim that we can therefore directly petition the Saints for prayer. This is why my position on petitioning the Saints for prayer is the following: We should, in directing our prayers to God, ask Him that He have the Saints in Heaven pray for us. I do not think there is anything objectionable to that, and even the Roman Catholic apologist Trent Horn has offered that as an appropriate approach (he apparently got that from me! haha).
