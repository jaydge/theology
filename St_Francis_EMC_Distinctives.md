## 2. Scripture & Authority

- **Affirms sola scriptura** via Article 6: Scripture "containeth all things necessary to salvation" — material sufficiency. **[Stated, A101-VI, III]**  
- Scripture is the **ultimate authority, infallible and inerrant**; everything measured against it; inerrancy allows for generalization/approximation. **[Stated, A101-V, VI]**  
- **Apocrypha:** mediating Article 6 position — read "for example of life and instruction of manners," never to establish doctrine. **[Stated, A101-VI]**
- **Deuterocanon, sharpened (RC-4).** Prefers "deuterocanon" to "Apocrypha." Reads Article 6 as NOT denying the deuterocanon's canonicity ("we're not saying that it's not canonical"), treating canonicity as open/uncertain rather than excluded, diverging from WCF 1.3 (definitively not canon). The Second Book of Homilies (Almsdeeds) cites Tobit and Sirach as places where "the Holy Ghost … teach[es]"; he uses this Homily positively. A mild internal wrinkle: a Homily calling Tobit Holy-Ghost-inspired sits against Article 6's own "not to establish any doctrine" clause. His settled "Via Media" position sits between evangelical rejection (Chick-tract "demonic") and the Roman "exactly the same as the rest of Scripture": the deuterocanon is questioned by church history (names Jerome and Pope Gregory the Great as rejecting it), used in worship and teaching for morals and example, not doctrine-establishing, and handled with critical-scholarly care (dates 2 Esdras 1-2 as post-Christian, refuses weak prooftexts). **[Stated, RC4-1, RC4-3, RC4-4, RC4-6, RC4-7]**
  - **[Analysis]** The functional core (no doctrine from the deuterocanon alone) is shared with the Reformed position; the real gap is narrow: open canonicity, terminology, and the Tobit-inspired Homily. See QC-e for the relay form of this question.  
- **Tradition does not replace or supersede Scripture**; it transmits Scripture and supplies interpretive context. Two "red flags" for any reading: (1) opposed by the earliest generations; (2) opposed by patristic consensus. Private opinion doesn't overrule consensus, but the fathers are a *check on interpretation*, not an independent doctrinal source. **[Stated, A101-VI]**  
- **Church not infallible** but has authority (Art. 20): may decree rites and settle controversies, revisable, never contrary to Scripture, never binding anything beyond Scripture for salvation. Keys (Matt 16/18) = church discipline, given to all apostles + successors, not Peter alone; Matt 16:18 = Peter's *confession* (Augustine, Ambrose, Athanasius). **[Stated, A101-V]**  
- **No total apostasy ever**: "we can reform, elucidate, contextualize — but never innovate" (Jude 3). The Spirit gives no new doctrine. **[Stated, A101-VI]**  
- ⚠️ **Operative-authority gap [Analysis]:** in *practice* his worship/ceremonial decisions run heavily on tradition — the Sarum Rite, the Fathers, "the historic church" — even where his stated principle is Scripture's sufficiency. Tradition functions as a near-coordinate authority in deed (filling space where Scripture is silent or, on his view, where a type is fulfilled), more than his stated doctrine of sola scriptura would suggest. This is the seam where the icons/ceremonial questions actually bite — see §11 and Open Questions.  
- **Doctrine of the fathers (ANF, RJ's own framing):** Christianity is "a received religion" — no generation without Christians (Matt 16:18); if we can't trust the fathers to transmit doctrine, we can't trust them to have transmitted the *Scriptures* they copied. The fathers are *closer to the apostles* (the great-great-grandfather analogy) and culturally fluent, so they're a "guide back to Christ and the apostles." But explicitly: **"not everything they say is true or even taught by all"** — flag idiosyncratic claims (Tertullian, Origen), and weigh especially the points of *universal consensus*. Where one's reading of Scripture cuts against a genuine patristic consensus, "be humble enough to be willing to submit our own interpretation." **[Stated, ANF-1]**  
  - **[Analysis]** This is a *high-consensus-Protestant* doctrine of tradition, not a Roman two-source one: the fathers check interpretation and carry heavy presumptive weight, but Scripture remains the only infallible norm and the fathers can err. It's close to (though slightly "higher" than) the Reformed *norma normata* view of the consensus patrum. The live tension is purely operative — see §3 and the Councils breakthrough.  
  - RJ's own most revealing question to a guest (ANF-4): *"What would you say to someone who reads Ignatius or Clement and says, 'I think he's a sincere Christian, but my reading of Scripture really goes against what he's saying'?"* — i.e., RJ himself names the sola-scriptura-vs-consensus problem as *the* major issue. He clearly feels its weight. **[Stated, ANF-4]**
- **Formulary-binding gating finding (RC-1, ★★ GATING, controls the whole Bucket A strategy).** On whether the Thirty-Nine Articles bind him: "the United States never adopted the thirty-nine articles as a required standard of faith"; as a deacon he was "never required to hold to" them and his bishop "never demands" it; he holds "most … arguably all … if I understand some of them in a certain way." He gives a concrete instance of his "the formularies self-contradict" hedge (cf. the IP-era note below): one Article's TITLE "contradicts the … content of that article" (unnamed). **Calibration for the internal-lever strategy:** for RJ the Articles bind by affirmation with interpretive latitude, not juridical subscription, so frame any Bucket A lever as "your formularies / what you affirm," not "a required subscription." **[Stated, RC1-7]**
  - **[Analysis]** Companion finding (RC1-8): the 16th-century formularies are, on his account, not frozen or infallible. The Reformers "themselves said … they're not infallible" and "you can change these things," so binding people to a 16th-century ceiling treats the Reformation like an infallible council; bishops set provincial standards. This is the same private-judgment-over-which-standards-bind structure he uses on conciliar selectivity (§3, Q5).
- **Sola scriptura as ecclesial, not solo (RC-5).** "I'm submitted to Scripture, I'm submitted to my bishop, I'm submitted to the Church and her teachings," rejecting the "you're your own pope" jab as a tu quoque; also defends sola scriptura against the "33,000 denominations" argument by debunking the count. **[Stated, RC5-3, RC5-4]**
- **Scripture and the councils (RC-7).** "As a traditionalist Anglican I hold to both Scripture and the ecumenical councils as authoritative," with Scripture the higher shared standard that proves the councils (*norma normata*; cf. creeds held because "proven most assuredly by scripture," Article 8, RC3-15). **[Stated, RC7-2]**  
- **Sola scriptura in operation (Rev) — two confirming data points.** (1) **"No dogmatizing beyond warrant."** Teaching the four living creatures as the four Gospels (the Irenaeus/Jerome/Augustine tradition), RJ embraces it as *helpful tradition* — "it comes to us from tradition… we don't treat it on the level of scripture" — but explicitly refuses to bind it: *"we never want to dogmatize something that we don't have the warrant to dogmatize from scripture."* A clean, real-time instance of his stated *norma normata* posture: tradition illumines, Scripture norms, and he polices the line. (2) **Sola scriptura is not presuppositionless biblicism.** He explicitly warns against the "the only thing you look at is scripture and your cultural presuppositions magically wash away" caricature — "that's not how the reformers approached scripture" — and insists on reading each text in its genre and historical-cultural setting (exegesis, not isogesis), with the fathers as interpretive guides. A mature, defensible account of sola scriptura that a Reformed reader shares. **[Stated, Rev-3, Rev-6]**  
  - **[Analysis]** Both data points cut *for* RJ's stated principle and slightly *against* the operative-authority worry — on doctrine and interpretation he genuinely operates as a high-consensus Protestant (*norma normata*). This makes the operative-authority gap (above) look increasingly **localized to ceremonial/worship practice** rather than doctrine: he won't dogmatize the four-creatures reading without scriptural warrant, yet he *will* install literal incense and an altar on the strength of an apocalyptic vision he otherwise reads symbolically (§13). That asymmetry — rigorous about not over-reading Scripture for *doctrine*, looser about deriving *physical worship practice* from symbolic visions — is itself worth a gentle, admiring observation in conversation: it shows the seam is not "he's a crypto-Romanist on authority" but "his ceremonial outruns the hermeneutic he applies everywhere else."

- **Hermeneutics / method (GV batch).** Operates a patristic-consensus "1500 years" hermeneutic, treating Zwinglian symbolism as a late novelty and citing Ignatius (d. 107) as an early witness (GV-18). Reads "this is my body" as an essence-realist, anti-metaphorical predication (object → predicate, resisting metaphor; Jesus as the archetypal "true … door"), a strongly literal sacramental exegesis (GV-20). Appeals selectively to *Reformed* formularies (Belgic, Westminster, LBCF) against memorialism (GV-19). Non-polemical on Celtic-vs-Roman historiography: rejects both an over-romanticized "pristine Celtic church" and an over-Romanized reading, around Whitby (GV-14). **[Stated/Stated-Analysis, GV-14, GV-18, GV-19, GV-20]**
  - **[Analysis]** Two consistency-check seams for the incense/Malachi thread: (1) GV-19's selectivity cuts both ways — the Belgic/Westminster/LBCF confessions he enlists against memorialism also reject his Lutheran sacramental union, so the appeal is selective; (2) GV-20's anti-metaphorical realism, applied consistently to Malachi 1:11 ("pure offering") and Revelation 5:8 (incense = prayer), is exactly the figurative-vs-literal pressure the Malachi reserve question turns on. Cross-ref Q1 / the Malachi reserve.
- 🎯 **The BCP-over-Articles interpretive master-move (GV-44, MAJOR method).** RJ states the Prayer Book communion service "is the lens by which we should interpret the articles … not the other way around." **[Stated, GV-44]** **[Analysis]** This is the master premise behind every narrow / title-vs-content reading of the anti-Roman Articles (cf. GV-43, §8). The Reformed counter: the Articles — the church's *subscribed confession* — norm the liturgy, not vice versa (the subscription tradition). This is the gating contest for the whole eucharistic/ceremonial lever set, and must be raised (Q0c/Q0d) *before* the content-anchored Article 28/29 question: if the liturgy is allowed to norm the Articles, the Article-29 content lever can be deflected through the communion-service lens. See the lens-priority gating question in the question list.
- **Authority locus: royal supremacy and provincial autonomy (GV-52, GV-53).** Royal supremacy is civil/protective, not doctrinal — the monarch is "a David character" who "cannot establish doctrine"; "we give not our princes the ministering … of God's word or of the sacraments"; and Article 37 (Civil Magistrates) "no longer applied to us in the United States, we actually changed it" (GV-52; edition note: the American Art. 37 differs from 1571/1662 — see the Formulary-Edition section). Ecclesiology is provincial-autonomy: the Church of England is not the Anglican Communion (a "confederacy"); US/Nigeria etc. are not under the crown; England is "first among equals" but "weakened … compromising"; RJ is "not at all submitted to the monarch"; and he rejects universal jurisdiction, papal supremacy, and papal infallibility (GV-53). **[Stated, GV-52, GV-53]**
  - **[Analysis]** GV-53 supplies the *positive* authority-locus the Q0c/Q0d scaffolding needed: authority is provincial/conciliar, consonant with Article 34's third clause (the particular/national church's power to ordain, change, and abolish ceremonies). This *gates* the Article-34 ceremonial lever on his own terms — by *whose* particular-church authority do incense, ad orientem, and pontifical vesture stand: the EMC's, or the Oxford Movement's? ## 3. Councils

- Website: "largely… the Seven Ecumenical Councils, with special emphasis on the first Four." **[Web]**  
- Positively invokes Councils 1–6 by name/content (Nicaea I, Constantinople I, Ephesus, Chalcedon, Constantinople II & III) for Trinitarian/Christological doctrine. **[Stated, A101-II, IV, AW-IV]**  
- 🎯 **BREAKTHROUGH (ANF-1):** RJ finally states his own position on Nicaea II. Asked whether councils are infallible, he says **no** — and adds, in his own words: *"certain ones like the Seventh Ecumenical Council, you're not required to… kiss icons or something. We wouldn't — we would say that's not a requirement, because that would force somebody into something they might not actually feel comfortable with and might feel like they're doing idolatry with it."* **[Stated, ANF-1]**  
  - **What this resolves:** he does **not** bind icon veneration on his people, and he explicitly grants that someone could reasonably regard it as idolatry. That is a substantially *Protestant* handling of Nicaea II and is consistent with Article 22 — it knocks out the strong "you must venerate" reading the trilemma feared.  
  - **What it leaves open:** he frames this as "not *required*" — which permits icon veneration as an option while not mandating it. He doesn't say it's *wrong*, only that it can't be *imposed*. He treats the Seventh Council the way he says Rome/EO treat canons in practice — selectively applied — rather than rejecting it outright. So the precise question shifts from "do you venerate?" to "**is veneration a legitimate option you'd defend, or merely one you won't impose?**" and "if the Seventh Council's anathemas against non-venerators are wrong, in what sense is it still 'ecumenical' for you?"  
- **Councils are not infallible** (his general view): he notes even Rome and EO hedge on conciliar infallibility in practice (popes move councils "in and out of authority"; EO bishops apply canons selectively), and says his own practical position is "virtually no different" — certain conciliar requirements simply aren't binding. **[Stated, ANF-1]**  
- **[Analysis]** This is the cleanest illustration yet of the operative-authority pattern (§2): Scripture and the first four/six councils are firm; the Seventh is held loosely and selectively, by *his* judgment of what may be imposed. That's a Protestant rule of faith in operation — and it's exactly the lever for the friendly version of question 1 (see Open Questions): not "you're too Catholic," but "by your own account you sit in private judgment over which conciliar canons bind — which is the very thing Rome says sola scriptura can't avoid."

### Papacy, gap FILLED (RC-7, RC-2, RC-1, RC-5)

- 🎯 **His positive papacy position, stated plainly (RC-7).** Peter and his Roman successors are "the first among equals … the position I hold to, as well as … the Eastern Orthodox"; this "would never allow … for papal supremacy or universal jurisdiction or papal infallibility." He GRANTS Peter was "the head of the Apostles" and a real Roman primacy, denying only the Vatican I dogmas: generous to Rome on primacy, firm on the dogmas. **[Stated, RC7-1, RC7-8, RC5-1]**
- **Exegetical basis.** Matthew 16's "rock" = Peter's confession, on the patristic consensus he cites (Cyprian, Augustine, who switched, Chrysostom, Cyril, Jerome, et al.); Ephesians 2:20 (apostles and prophets, Christ the cornerstone) names no Peter singled out. The keys / binding-and-loosing go to ALL the apostles (Matthew 18:18, plural) and concern discipline (an appellate structure), aligning with the Reformed account of the keys (cf. §2, Rev-2). **[Stated, RC7-4, RC5-1, RC7-5]**
- **Conciliar-historical case against papal supremacy.** The seven councils were not called or attended in person by the Bishop of Rome (legates only); Rome was not even invited to the second council, which was presided over by Meletius, himself in schism with Rome yet a Roman Catholic saint. Councils judged and condemned popes: the third judged Celestine; the fourth judged Leo's Tome and overrode the legates' attempt to "pull rank"; the fifth deposed Vigilius; the sixth anathematized Honorius as a heretic. Canons (Nicaea 6, Constantinople 2-3, Chalcedon 28) limit Rome's jurisdiction by name. Acts 15 grounds collegial authority (Peter speaks, James judges, v. 19). **[Stated, RC7-6, RC7-7, RC7-12]**
- **[Analysis]** His conciliarism is Article-21-compatible: councils are authoritative but under Scripture, and the Seventh is held loosely on principle, not arbitrarily (cf. the Nicaea II breakthrough above). This gives the existing "selectivity" open question (item 1, below) a principled basis rather than an ad hoc one.
- 🎯 **The burden-of-proof asymmetry (RC-5, ★★).** On the papacy he puts the burden squarely on the innovator: "they need to show that as biblical and as historical … we don't get to presuppose that … and then I have to prove it wrong" (a regulative-style burden). This is the **mirror image** of his incense burden (§13, RC3-9), where the burden runs the other way (retention presumed, exclusion needing a strong argument). A genuine internal-consistency lever; see QC-f. He also rejects "retcon" / "it could still be true technically" reasoning on the papacy; part of his own incense "it isn't negated" defense uses exactly that move. **[Stated/Analysis, RC5-6, RC5-7]**

Roots it in Aristotle/Plato under Scripture's authority, defending the use of Greek philosophical terms (logos; "in him we live and move"). **[Stated, A101-II, AW-VI, Misc-2025]**  
  - One sharp parishioner objection worth noting (Misc-2025): a layman pushed back that the image-of-God→theistic-personalism inference "doesn't pass the giggle test" (the Mona Lisa is an image of a beautiful woman but isn't one). RJ agreed and used it to reinforce his rejection of theistic personalism. Tells you the congregation includes at least one philosophically sharp interlocutor and that RJ welcomes the pushback. **[Stated, Misc-2025]**  
- Chalcedonian, dyothelite Christology; God suffers/dies *according to the human nature*. **[Stated, A101-II, IV]**  
- Filioque: affirms the theology, sympathizes with the objection to its insertion. **[Stated, A101-II]**  
- Islam "100% a false religion"; not the same God; not "brothers and sisters." **[Stated, A101-IV]**
- **Theotokos affirmed on christological grounds (RC-6).** "In giving birth to Jesus she gave birth to God because Jesus is God" (Ephesus / Chalcedon); standard anti-Nestorian and anti-Arian reasoning (rejects two-persons Christology; affirms the Son's eternal pre-existence). Confirms the high-Christology finding and gives Q16's assumed Theotokos premise a direct source. **[Stated, RC6-1, RC6-3]**
- **Heresy agreement across the Tiber (RC-7).** Agrees monophysitism, monothelitism, and the Three Chapters controversy (which he later self-corrects to Nestorianism, RC2-2) are heresies: "you as a Roman Catholic and I as an Anglican." **[Stated, RC7-9]**

## 5. Soteriology

- **Justification by faith alone — affirmed explicitly and repeatedly.** Saved by Christ alone, grace through faith, not works. Law/gospel framework central to his preaching ("the law is the diagnosis… the gospel is the cure"); the law is good (a functional third use); Rom 6 against antinomianism. **[Stated, A101 II/III/VII, AW-I/IV]**  
- **Imputation; simul iustus et peccator** (from the Lutherans). **[Stated, A101-VIII]**  
- **Sola fide confirmed back to the deacon era (RC-1).** Affirms the Reformation "recoveries": "salvation by grace alone, justification by faith alone"; calls Cranmer "a reformation martyr." Denies that the Church of England "accepted" the Synod of Dort as part of its tradition, resisting a hard-Calvinist / TULIP identity, consistent with the conditional-election lean below. Confirms robust sola fide is not a recent development but present from roughly 2020-2021 onward. **[Stated, RC1-15]**  
- Election: affirms election to salvation; **rejects election to damnation** and supralapsarianism; personally leans conditional but holds either acceptable. **[Stated, A101-IV]**  
- **Rejects once-saved-always-saved**; apostasy possible. Repentance and faith are gifts of God. **[Stated, A101-VII]**  
- Rome: "you can find the gospel in the Roman Catholic church," but its justification doctrine/practice trends transactional. **[Stated, A101-IV]**  
- Rejects dispensationalism and the rapture. **[Stated, A101-II]**  
- **Eschatology (Rev): postmillennial + partial preterist.** Personally holds **postmillennialism** ("this is the one that I hold to") and **partial preterism** — most of Revelation already fulfilled by ~AD 70 (the destruction of the Jerusalem temple), though *not* the general resurrection. Optimistic "upward trajectory" view of church history. Explicitly does **not** dogmatize it: pre-/a-/post-millennial are all "within the realm of orthodoxy," and his jurisdiction has not made any one a confessional requirement. Explicitly rejects **hyper-preterism** as denying the creeds and the bodily resurrection. Reinforces and extends the anti-dispensationalism above. Detail in §18. **[Stated, Rev-1, Rev-7]**  
  - **[Analysis]** This is genuinely new and substantially **Reformed-friendly**. Postmillennialism has deep Puritan/Reformed and Anglican pedigree (he names Puritans, Presbyterians, and Anglicans as its post-Reformation carriers), and partial preterism is held across confessional-Reformed circles. Nothing here is a cross-tradition fault line with a PCA/OPC reader — at most an *intramural* eschatological difference (many in the PCA/OPC are amillennial; some are postmil). Pairs naturally with his covenant theology and one-people-of-God reading (§17). Worth noting as common ground, and as a useful tonal datum: he is irenic about millennial disagreement, which models the register he'll expect in conversation.  
- **Atonement (COT):** affirms penal/substitutionary atonement as "completely undeniable in scripture" — but warns against *reducing* the atonement to penal substitution alone, citing 1 Cor 15 (no forgiveness without the resurrection). Explicitly says he thinks **Christus Victor** (Christ's conquest of sin and death) may be "the most important" facet, "competitive with penal substitution," because it's the undoing of the ultimate enemy and most magnifies God's glory. Frames the atonement as multifaceted and the whole Incarnation (not just the cross) as redemptive. **[Stated, COT-4]**  
  - **[Analysis]** This is the one place his soteriology is audibly *not* standard confessional-Reformed. The Westminster tradition treats penal substitution as the central, organizing category of the atonement, with Christus Victor as a true but subordinate consequence of it. RJ inverts the emphasis. It's not heterodox — penal substitution is affirmed — but a PCA/OPC reader will hear the de-prioritizing of penal substitution as a meaningful divergence, and it's worth a gentle question about *ordering* (is Christ's victory the ground of, or the result of, the satisfaction of divine justice?). This is a genuine cross-tradition difference, not an internal-Anglican one.  
- **Dispensationalism (COT):** calls it "a very errant belief, I would say heretical" — two-peoples-of-God (church + ethnic Israel), with extreme forms allowing salvation apart from Christ; "flies in the face of Galatians." Strongly covenantal/one-people-of-God reading (Gentiles grafted into the same tree, Rom 11; heirs of Abraham via union with Christ, Gal 3). Reformed-friendly. **[Stated, COT-5]**

- **Anti-Roman on justification, deacon-era (GV-1, GV-22).** "Reject the Roman understanding of justification, definitely" (GV-1); self-distances from Rome generally, agreeing with roughly three-quarters of standard anti-Catholic critiques and noting "I'm not even Catholic" (GV-22). **[Stated, GV-1; Stated-Analysis, GV-22]** **[Analysis]** Confirms the anti-Tridentine premise the Malachi 1:11 "pure offering" reserve depends on — he would indeed reject the Tridentine-adjacent conclusion that lever generates.
- **Objective assurance grounded in promise, not psychology (GV-5).** Assurance must rest on "a promise given … to me, rather than a … psychological state within myself" — on baptism, not introspective/subjective faith. **[Stated, GV-5]** **[Analysis]** Bucket-B (cross-tradition; vs the Reformed introspective-plus-Spirit's-witness account, WCF 18). Low ceremonial relevance; coheres with the BP-29 / D10 assurance datum (§7).
- **Apostasy of the genuinely regenerate (GV-6).** Affirms real apostasy is possible for the genuinely regenerate; distinguishes mere faithlessness (2 Tim 2:13) from apostasy; rejects both Lordship Salvation and cheap grace. **[Stated, GV-6]** **[Analysis]** Bucket-B, and Article 16 ("Of Sin after Baptism") is *compatible* with this — **not** a Bucket-A lever. Coheres with the COT-2 / Rev-5/8 apostasy cluster (§7).

## 6. Sacraments — Number & Definition

- Augustine/Article 25 definition; sacraments are **God's promises, not our works** (engagement-ring analogy). **[Stated, A101-VII]**  
- **Two-tier, not flat seven:** two *dominical* (baptism, Holy Communion) instituted by Christ with forgiveness attached; five *ecclesial* (confirmation, penance/absolution, holy orders, matrimony, unction) sacraments "in a lesser sense" per the Homilies — the church may modify their signs but not their realities. **[Stated, A101-VII, VIII]**
- **Realist sacramentology, confirmed (BP-11).** Sacraments communicate grace, "rooted in the incarnation itself"; "to receive a sacrament is to be in some way united to Christ." **[Stated, BP-Sac]**
- **His own four-fold validity framework (BP-12), structural.** Proper minister, proper matter, proper form, proper intent — "if any of these four are wrong, the sacrament itself cannot be assured of its validity." Grants an ecumenical floor: "your average evangelical trinitarian church will get it right" on most of the four. This is the scaffolding behind the orders-validity-for-Eucharist finding below (§11, BP-15). **[Stated, BP-Sac]**
- **Matter-validity specifics (BP-17) [low weight].** Welch's-type grape juice invalid for communion (additives "change its nature"); wine may be non-alcoholic if preserved without additives; leavened/unleavened bread both valid. **[Stated, BP-Sac]**
- **Seven sacraments, unqualified affirmation (BP-27).** "We as Anglicans do believe in seven sacraments," set inside an Incarnation-grounded sacramentology ("Jesus is the ultimate sacrament"). Confirms IP-4 and strengthens Q7 (seven sacraments vs. Article 25) in his own voice. **[Stated, BP-Angl]**

- **Confirmation and absolution as "sacraments" (GV-24, GV-39) — Article 25 lever.** Calls confirmation "important … essential … one of the sacraments" (GV-24), and lists the means of grace as operating "normatively … through baptism, through the eucharist, through absolution etc." (GV-39). So his working sacramental set treats confirmation and absolution as grace-bearing sacraments. **[Stated, GV-24, GV-39]** **[Analysis] New Bucket-A lever (Article 25).** Article 25 places confirmation and penance among the "five commonly called Sacraments" that "are not to be counted for Sacraments of the Gospel" and "have not like nature of Sacraments with Baptism, and the Lord's Supper." Pairs with the unqualified seven-sacraments affirmation (BP-27 above) and is sharpened by GV-23 (same GV set cites the Articles favorably as his own standard — §7). Pre-empt the "broad/lesser-sense" retort by pinning to Art. 25's explicit denial of "like nature." Operationalized as QA-Art25 in the question list; reinforces Known-Gaps G7.

## 7. Baptism

- **Baptismal regeneration affirmed strongly** — "I cannot think of a single early Church Father who denies" it; regeneration normatively tied to baptism, possible before/outside it. Unites to Christ's death (Rom 6); forgives sins past/present/future (quasi-atemporal); washing of regeneration; "baptism saves." Infant baptism affirmed; confirmation makes the faith one's own. Compatible with sola fide because baptism is God's promise received, not man's work; claims reformers held a *stronger* view than Rome's "second plank." **[Stated, A101-VI, VII, VIII]**  
- **Identity-defining, confirmed back to the deacon era (RC-1).** Baptismal regeneration is, in his words, "explicitly in our formularies": "the sacrament of baptism is regenerative" / "baptism regenerates." It functions for him as part of the dividing line between "Catholic" and "watered down" / nondenominational Christianity. Confirms the IP-5 finding back to roughly 2020-2021 and shows it is non-negotiable for him, not a recent development. **[Stated, RC1-4]**  
- Holy-water stoup at the door: dip fingers, sign of the cross, "remember your baptism" (cites Luther). **[Stated, Recon-Euch]**  
- **Baptism + apostasy, refined (COT):** baptism "objectively" grafts onto Christ (Rom 6; 1 Pet 3:21 — the flood as the *figure*, baptism the greater reality). But he carefully distinguishes this from "losing your salvation": he says you *cannot* lose salvation, because apostasy is not accidental — it's "an intentional, full-throated… actual hatred of Christ" by which one throws away the ring (the engagement-ring/Steak-'n-Shake-thrown-ring image again). So a baptized person who apostatizes "has walked away from that regeneration." **[Stated, COT-2]**  
- **Apostasy restated (Rev) — confirms COT-2, same structure.** Teaching the Rom 11 olive tree (Rev 7 / the 144,000), RJ again distinguishes "stumbling/sinning" (which does *not* forfeit salvation) from "an active, willful, belligerent apostasizing" which is being "cut off" — and grounds it in Hebrews 6/10 and the warning passages, plus the 1 John 2:19 "they went out from us because they were not of us" category for those who only *seemed* to belong. He adds a careful anti-synergism guard on Phil 2:12–13 ("I don't want to make it sound synergistic… it is God who works in you"). No new movement from COT-2; it deepens and re-confirms the same FV-adjacent shape from a second class. **[Stated, Rev-5, Rev-8]**  
  - **[Analysis]** This is the cluster a PCA/OPC reader will scrutinize hardest: objective baptismal grafting + real (if deliberate) apostasy of the regenerate is exactly the conceptual neighborhood of **Federal Vision** (objective covenant membership conferred at baptism, forfeitable by covenant-breaking). RJ is *not* self-consciously FV and his vocabulary is more Lutheran/Prayer-Book than Wilkins/Wright, but the structure overlaps. His "you can't *lose* it, only *throw it away*" move is doing real work — it's how he reconciles baptismal regeneration with perseverance language. Worth probing how he relates this to the Reformed distinction between the visible and invisible church, and to WCF 17 on perseverance. **Note for framing the conversation:** my disagreement here is precisely about **baptismal regeneration and baptismal objectivity** (the FV-adjacent structure), *not* about the mode or proper subjects of baptism — I'm open to the credo-baptist (LBCF 1689) view of mode/subjects, so infant baptism per se isn't the fault line. Keep the pressure on regeneration-and-objectivity, not on paedo- vs. credo-baptism. This is the single most important place for the FV/NPP/justification paper to sharpen the final questions.
- **Indelible sacramental character (BP-13).** "Once you are baptized, you can never be unbaptized. Once you are ordained, you can never be unordained"; defrocking removes "rights to operate," not orders. The baptism half coheres with the objective-grafting + reversible-forfeiture frame already on record (COT-2, IP-5): one cannot be UN-baptized, only walk away from grace given. **[Stated, BP-Sac]**
- **Three confirming threads (BP-14), aligning with Article 26 and IP-5/IP-6.** (a) Anti-Donatist: "Sin, heresy, and even apostasy on the part of the minister do not in and of themselves invalidate your baptism. Baptism is God's promise, not the minister's." (b) Objectivity restated: "the central component of it is not your declaration of faith. Rather, it is the declaration from God that you are his." (c) Permissive minister: any human being, "male or female, Christian or non-Christian," can baptize validly. **[Stated, BP-Sac]**
- **Compact triple confirmation, post-conversion candor (BP-21).** "Baptism actually does save" (regeneration, confirming the above); "this is the body and blood of Christ, it actually really truly is" plus must "physically be there to physically receive" (real presence + anti-virtual communion, §8/§10); "liturgy is about worshiping God not worshiping ourselves" (§13). **[Stated, BP-Switch]**
- **Apostasy view restated against an online interlocutor (BP-22).** Confirms the §7/§5 apostasy position and FV-adjacent frame already on record (COT-2, IP-5): "one can walk away from the faith… apostasy… it's a real thing," against a critic who called this view a denial of Christ. **[Stated, BP-Switch]**
- **Infant baptism — identity-required, individual obligation softer (BP-24) [low weight, bears on D6].** Accepting infant baptism is required for Anglican identity ("as Anglicans we have to be okay with infant baptism"), but the individual obligation to baptize one's own infant is softer than strict: "it'd be really weird to not baptize your infants," rather than sinful or invalid not to. **[Stated, BP-Switch]**
- **Sola fide vs. regeneration-of-all, both halves on record from one source (BP-28).** Guards sacraments against works-salvation: "these are not works… the sacraments are the works of God"; conditions Eucharistic benefit on faith via 1 Cor 11 ("if these are done apart from faith these are actually to our condemnation"). For baptism specifically he leans hard on objectivity instead ("nothing can take away that I've been baptized… it's God's decision"). Does not resolve the existing Q9 sola-fide/regeneration-of-all coherence seam (IP-5/IP-8); puts both halves on record in one breath. **[Stated, BP-Angl]**
- **Assurance grounded in objective baptism (BP-29) — fills D10, previously silent in the corpus.** Against a recounted adolescent crisis under sinner's-prayer-plus-OSAS theology (re-praying, "afraid I didn't mean it enough"): "the sacraments provide… an objective external assurance." Looks to his own (Baptist) baptism, not a decision, as the ground: "I am a Christian because I've been… baptized rather than because I prayed a prayer… it's God's decision." Honors his Baptist baptism as fully valid — not rebaptized upon becoming Anglican, consistent with BP-14's anti-Donatism. **[Stated, BP-Angl]**
  - **[Analysis]** Tension worth holding, not pressing as a gotcha: if the baptized can still deliberately apostatize (above, BP-22), the bare fact of baptism is a structurally odd sole ground for assurance — contrast WCF 18 (assurance grounded in the promises, the inward evidences of grace, and the Spirit's witness, not the sacramental fact alone). Folded into D10 in the question list.

- **Baptismal regeneration, GV set — terminus-not-trigger refinement (GV-4, GV-26).** Regeneration is "definitely received" by valid baptism (GV-4); but he also holds "regeneration has to precede Faith" and is not necessarily tied to baptism's *moment* ("could take place anytime"), so baptism is the terminus by which regeneration is received rather than a strict ex-opere trigger. An earlier (2020) statement is stronger-causation: baptism "gives you that faith and repentance … is actually the actual act of repentance" (GV-26). **[Stated, GV-4, GV-26]** **[Analysis]** Read developmentally: 2020's strong causal language (GV-26) → the later careful uncoupling of regeneration from the baptismal moment (GV-4). Softer than ex opere operato; coheres with the COT-2 "objective grafting + reversible forfeiture" frame.
- **Baptism "brings salvation," grounded in the Articles (GV-23).** Baptism "brings you that salvation"; it is "not a work … a gift from God that you receive"; "an actual effectual sign as it says in the thirty-nine articles." **[Stated, GV-23]** **[Analysis] Bucket-A META, feeds Q0c/Q0d.** He cites the Thirty-Nine Articles as his own binding standard when it suits him (here, to ground baptismal efficacy) — useful for closing the "the Articles don't bind me" escape on the *other* levers: he treats them as authoritative where convenient.
- 🚫 **DO-NOT-DEPLOY (GV-38): baptismal regeneration is the formulary position, and he is faithful to it.** RJ reads Article 27 at length and the 1662 liturgy ("regenerate and born anew of water and the Holy Ghost"), calls baptismal regeneration "an Anglican thing … explicitly in our formularies," notes even Calvin held a form of it (Inst. 4.15), and rejects the "merely covenantal … watered-down Reformed Baptist" view. **[Stated, GV-38]** **[Analysis]** Here RJ is *formulary-faithful*: Article 27 and the 1662 liturgy support him, and the strict-Westminster denial is the side that strains against the Anglican formularies (cf. Gorham). **Do not run baptismal regeneration as a Bucket-A lever** — the real Bucket-A eucharistic leverage is Article 28/29, not baptism. Keep the live disagreement on regeneration/objectivity as Bucket-B / cross-tradition (per the §7 top note).
- **Covenantal paedobaptism — common ground (GV-27).** Reformed covenantal rationale: circumcision-baptism continuity (Col 2:11-12), household baptisms, "a widening not a replacing" (Rom 9-11), anti-dispensationalism. **[Stated-Analysis, GV-27]** **[Analysis] Bucket-B COMMON GROUND** (Article 27 consonant; coheres with §17 covenant theology) — **not** a lever. Credit it.

## 8. Eucharist — Presence

- **Real, substantial presence**; bread and wine *remain*. Reads John 6:52–58 eucharistically (with Calvin, against Luther). **[Stated, A101-VII, AW-V, Recon-Euch]**  
- **Two views both "acceptable" in Anglicanism, both in the formularies:** (a) Calvin/Vermigli — the faithful alone receive Christ; (b) Lutheran — the wicked also receive the body, to their condemnation. **RJ leans firmly Lutheran**: "I lean much more towards the Luther understanding." Notes Cranmer personally moved transubstantiation → Lutheran → Calvinist, "encompassing the debate as one person." **[Stated, AW-V, A101-VII]**  
- On the "body, blood, soul, divinity" phrase: RJ uses it to describe the **shared overlap** among Rome/Lutheran/Anglican/Reformed ("all of us would confess… Christ is truly present, his body, blood, soul, divinity are present"), then immediately **qualifies**: Christ is **not locally present** (cites Aquinas — moving a consecrated chalice doesn't move Christ), the differences are "philosophical," and "we should say… in receiving the bread and the wine we are receiving the body and blood of Christ." So the phrase is his summary of the catholic consensus he claims, not a Tridentine concomitance claim he is personally pressing. **[Stated, Recon-Euch — see Cross-Batch note 1]**  
- **Rejects transubstantiation** — not "damnable," but "overthrows the nature of a sacrament" (Art. 28) by destroying bread and wine. Some Anglicans hold it; he doesn't. **[Stated, A101-III/VII, AW-V]**  
- **Rejects mere memorialism** "pretty universally." **[Stated, A101-VII]**  
- Worthy reception: trinitarian baptism; no habitual unrepented sin; basic Nicene orthodoxy; belief in *some* form of real presence (softened pastorally — settled rejection is the problem, not weak faith). Open communion to baptized creedal Christians; rejected Eastern Orthodoxy in part over closed communion. **[Stated, A101-VII, I]**
- **Identity-defining real presence, deacon era (RC-1).** Real presence ("the real true body and blood of our Lord") is identity-defining for him; distinguishes Anglican "real objective presence" from transubstantiation and rejects transubstantiation. Reads Calvin as "more catholic" / higher than the modern Reformed and "even than the Westminster Confession," and "stronger on the Eucharist." **[Stated, RC1-3, RC1-4, RC1-9]**
- **Mass-teaching detail, 2025 (RC-3).** Ties the mixed chalice to real presence plus John 19:34; "we don't believe in transubstantiation, but we agree with the Lutherans, and even Calvinists in many ways"; embraces the dual words of administration as deliberately Lutheran (objective: "the body … preserve thy body") plus Calvinist (faith: "feed on him in thy heart by faith"); affirms a high presence ("body, blood, soul, divinity," concomitance / Tridentine phrasing) but EXPLICITLY non-local (cites Aquinas: moving the chalice does not move Christ); affirms concomitance yet always offers both kinds. Confirms the §8 Lutheran-lean finding and IP-7; the non-local qualifier is Article-28-compatible (heavenly and spiritual manner), so the friction for Q3/Q4 sits in the maximal phrasing and the reverence practices, not the presence doctrine itself. **[Stated, RC3-16, RC3-17, RC3-24, RC3-25, RC3-26]**
- **Rejects memorialism as a misreading of the fathers (RC-5).** Names MacArthur's Master's Seminary for quote-mining Augustine, Origen, Tertullian, Cyprian, and Cyril to construct a memorialist patristic case; sources his own patristic real-presence reading and distances him from low-church Reformed/Baptist memorialism. **[Stated, RC5-2]**

- **Pontifical vesture (mitre, cope, crozier) is historically distinct from, and later than, the everyday eucharistic vestments (alb/chasuble/stole) already logged above.** The alb/chasuble trace to ordinary Greco-Roman civilian dress retained by clergy after secular fashion moved on (roughly 4th-9th c.); the mitre has no attestation before the 10th c. and does not become standard episcopal headgear until the 11th-12th c., with no patristic or scriptural warrant at all; the cope derives from an outdoor processional cape (pluviale) entering formal liturgical use roughly 10th-12th c. **[External, EXT-1]**

- **Full pontifical vesture was abolished in the English Reformation and absent for roughly three centuries, then reintroduced by the 19th-c. Ritualist movement.** The 1552 BCP rubric reduced clergy to the surplice alone; a direct exercise of the Article 34 authority RJ affirms (the national Church's power to abolish ceremonies). Reintroduction begins with the Ritualist movement from the 1850s-1870s, a self-consciously Romanizing/Sarum-revivalist party, not a continuous practice. **[External, EXT-1]**
  - **[Analysis]** Sharper than the existing chasuble-as-warm-garment material: the mitre specifically has no claim to antiquity at all (medieval invention, contemporaneous with the centralizing juridical papacy), making the "pan-catholic, also in the East" rebuttal (RC1-12, logged above) weaker for pontificals than for the chasuble, since the mitre isn't even ancient in the West. The reintroduction-after-abolition framing engages Article 34 on its abolition clause specifically, not only its retention clause.

- **The Ritualist controversy's actual mechanism, corrected (260620 research pass).** An earlier draft overstated episcopal unity against the revival ("against the wishes of the bishops") and has been corrected. Accurate picture: individual parish priests asserted a contested legal reading of the Ornaments Rubric (1857-66 saw "considerable extension" of vestments/incense "while the claim was generally made that they were all perfectly lawful"); the episcopate was genuinely divided, not unified in opposition (Evangelical bishops like Waldegrave and Bickersteth vocally hostile, calling vestments "Romeward" and tied to "Romish error," while other bishops were sympathetic); enforcement was attempted civilly via the Public Worship Regulation Act 1874 and largely failed (the 1877 Ridsdale judgment, four imprisoned clergy 1878-81, ongoing litigation and unrest); some bishops actively used discretionary veto power to shield Ritualist clergy from prosecution under the Act even when personally unsympathetic, so prosecutions were "eventually abandoned through use of the episcopal veto." **[External, EXT-1]**
  - **[Analysis] Reframe for Q8a.** The corrected mechanism is a better lever than the original overstated one. Shifts the question from "bishops disapproved and were overridden" (a claim about persons) to a structural point Article 34 is actually about: ceremonial authority properly sits in the national Church's corporate power to ordain/change/abolish, not in individual parish clergy's private judgment about contested rubrics. The Ritualist revival is a textbook case of clergy exploiting a jurisdictional/legal gap to reintroduce corporately-abolished ceremony, rather than receiving clear corporate re-authorization.   - **The sharpened question (what OQ8 now has to do).** The lever still holds, but it has to be aimed at the *seam between his two warrants*, not just the OT one. Three moves, in ascending order of force:  
    1. **The signification problem.** RJ himself says the heavenly incense *is* a symbol — Rev 5:8 and 8:3–4 *interpret* the bowls/smoke as "the prayers of the saints," and he affirms "incense is all throughout scripture symbolic of prayer." So Revelation gives him the *thing signified* (the prayers of the saints ascending) and the *interpretation of the symbol* — it does not obviously command the *physical signifier* (literal burning incense) in the earthly assembly. The heavenly altar in Rev 8 has no human celebrant lighting a thurible; the censer is in an angel's (or Christ's) hand. By what warrant does "heaven displays the reality that prayer ascends like incense" license "therefore we burn the physical substance"? This is the cleanest pressure point and it is *internal*: he reads the bowls symbolically, then materializes the symbol.  
    2. **The apocalyptic-genre problem (uses his own hermeneutic again).** He insists, correctly and repeatedly, that Revelation's imagery is *not* "woodenly literal" — the sea of glass, the slain Lamb with seven horns and seven eyes, the 24 elders, the living creatures, the bowls, the golden altar are all symbolic/typological, read through their scriptural interpretation. He will not build a literal sea of glass or seat 24 literal elders. So *why is the incense the one element of the heavenly furniture that gets reproduced as a literal physical object on earth,* when every other item in the same vision he treats as symbol pointing to a reality? The selectivity is the tension: a consistent application of his own anti-literalist apocalyptic hermeneutic would read the heavenly incense as it reads the heavenly sea — as a symbol of a reality (prayer, peace) the church *has in Christ*, not as a blueprint for sanctuary furniture.  
    3. **The fulfillment problem, restated to survive the escape hatch.** Even granting heavenly-pattern participation, his COT rule was about *which* OT shadows are fulfilled-and-retired. He typologizes the tabernacle altar of incense as a shadow of "the prayers of the saints" and ultimately of Christ's intercession (Heb 7–10; cf. §17, where Christ "daily presents himself before the Father interceding"). Revelation 8 *confirms* that typology — the heavenly reality is intercession/prayer, mediated by the angel-priest who is plausibly Christ (RJ's own read). So the heavenly scene, on his interpretation, points to *Christ's heavenly intercession and the saints' prayers* — exactly the fulfilled reality Hebrews says replaces the shadow. Reviving the *shadow's physical apparatus* (altar + incense) in the earthly assembly, when the vision itself directs us to the *fulfilled reality*, is the move his Hebrews argument forbids — now with Revelation as a witness *for* the fulfillment reading rather than against it.  
  - **[Analysis — for JD]** "Heavenly-pattern participation" is a real and respectable category, and it is *not* uniquely Eastern Orthodox, though the EO press it hardest (the Divine Liturgy as participation in the heavenly liturgy; the iconostasis as the heaven-earth boundary). It also has Roman, Lutheran, and high-Anglican forms, and there is a *thin* version even the Reformed grant. **How the classically Reformed tradition handles it** (so you can engage it without either conceding it or attacking a legitimate idea):  
    - *The Reformed do affirm a real participation.* Calvin's eucharistic doctrine is built on *sursum corda* — by the Spirit the worshipper is *lifted up to heaven* to feed on the ascended Christ, rather than Christ being brought down to the elements (*Institutes* 4.17.31–36). WCF 21 and the Heidelberg Catechism assume the gathered church joins the worship of the church triumphant. Hebrews 12:22–24 ("you have come to Mount Zion… to the assembly of the firstborn… to innumerable angels") is the Reformed proof-text that earthly worship *already* participates in the heavenly. So the Reformed answer to RJ is **not** "there is no heavenly participation" — that would be conceding the field on a point Scripture teaches. The disagreement is about **what participation licenses.**  
    - *The Reformed limit is the same one that governs the whole RPW: participation transmits the heavenly* ***realities*** *(access to God, communion with Christ and the saints, the prayers ascending, the "holy holy holy"), not a mandate to* ***reproduce the heavenly furniture as physical objects.*** The Reformed read Hebrews as teaching that the *true* tabernacle is heaven itself (Heb 8:1–5, 9:11–24) and that the earthly copies were shadows now fulfilled — so the direction of fulfillment runs *away from* re-materializing altars and incense, not *toward* it. To participate in the heavenly worship is to do by faith and the Spirit what heaven does in reality; it is *not* to build a scale model of heaven's sanctuary. The Sanctus is the test case the Reformed happily accept: we *sing* the "holy, holy, holy" with the angels (a reality-participation in the praise) — we do not therefore install a literal sea of glass, six-winged creatures, or 24 thrones. Incense is the item where RJ crosses from participating-in-the-reality to reproducing-the-furniture, and that crossing is exactly what the genre (symbol, not blueprint) and the fulfillment hermeneutic (shadow→reality, not reality→new-shadow) both forbid.  
    - *The element/circumstance distinction does the same work from the other side.* Even if heaven "uses incense," heaven's worship is not the *regulative authority* for the elements of new-covenant gathered worship — Christ's institution and apostolic practice are (WCF 21.1). The NT descriptions of gathered worship (Acts 2:42; 1 Cor 11–14; Col 3:16; Eph 5:19; 1 Tim 2) prescribe Word, prayer, sacrament, psalmody — and contain *no* instituted incense, altar-sacrifice, or thurifer for the assembly. A vision of heaven is not a church order. (This is the point where his "Jesus at the synagogue / Hanukkah" examples also fail: descriptive, not prescriptive; and the synagogue is itself a circumstance.)  
    - **Net for the conversation:** don't deny heavenly-pattern participation — affirm it warmly (Heb 12:22–24; Calvin's *sursum corda*), then locate the disagreement precisely: *participation in the heavenly reality is granted; the question is whether the vision of heaven's symbolic furniture is a* ***prescription to reproduce that furniture physically*** *in the earthly assembly — and on your own apocalyptic hermeneutic (symbol, not camcorder) and your own Hebrews/COT fulfillment hermeneutic (shadow→fulfilled reality), it isn't.* That keeps you from attacking something legitimate while still landing the internal-consistency point. This is the single strongest line in the corpus and the Revelation class both sharpened it and told you exactly which counter he'll reach for.  
    - **Sources to pull from [Analysis, added v0.8]:** the keystone is **John Owen, *An Exposition of the Epistle to the Hebrews* (chs. 8–10)** — the new-covenant priest, sacrifice, tabernacle, and altar are all *heavenly* (Christ's), and the heavenly realities could not be introduced without the *removal* of the earthly types: the precise answer to "heaven has an altar, so we have one." The closest *historical* precedent is **intra-Anglican**: the Laudian "altar wars" of the 1630s, where the avant-garde (Andrewes, Cosin, Laud) reproduced heaven's throne-room in the chancel — at Durham, Cosin's gilded star-painted ceiling and ranks of carved angels — on a "beauty of holiness / gateway to heaven" rationale, and conforming Reformed Anglicans objected (**Peter Smart**, Durham, 7 July 1628, deprived and imprisoned; **Prynne** and **Burton** vs. Cosin's *Collection of Private Devotions*). ## 17. Christ in the OT Class — Hermeneutic & Covenant Theology (2025, all RJ)

- **Method: christotelic typology.** The OT is read as a unified story pointing forward to Christ — shadow→substance (Moses *foreshadows*, Christ *casts the shadow*); the whole arc is "the undoing of the fall," Genesis 3 → Revelation 22; redemption = the rewinding of fall/flood/Babel. Heavy on *fulfillment* language (37×) and *type/shadow*. Reads explicitly Christological types throughout: the protoevangelium (Gen 3:15), the flood-as-baptism (1 Pet 3:21, baptism the *greater* reality), Passover lamb/blood-on-doorposts → cross + Eucharist, the bronze serpent (John 3:14), Melchizedek's bread-and-wine → Eucharist (Heb 7), Moses as prophet-priest-king → Christ, Joshua (=Yeshua) leading into the land, David as shepherd/warrior/psalmist/king → Christ. **[Stated, COT-1 through COT-7]**  
- **Covenant theology, one people of God:** Gentiles grafted into the same tree (Rom 11); heirs of Abraham's promises via union with Christ the true "seed" (Gal 3); promised land → all creation in the resurrection. This is the engine of his anti-dispensationalism. **[Stated, COT-5]**  
- **Creation:** *creatio ex nihilo* defended against "God merely formed pre-existing matter"; ties it to classical theism and divine aseity. Genesis 1:26 read as a hint of the Trinity. **[Stated, COT-1]**  
- **Law/gospel + union with Christ:** the OT sacrificial system instills "blood atonement is necessary for forgiveness"; Christ the final, non-repeatable sacrifice (Heb), who "daily presents himself before the Father interceding" (note: this intercession-language recurs and is doing the same work as his Eucharistic "re-presentation, not re-sacrifice" line — §9/§10). Salvation = union with Christ, received in *word and sacrament*, not "mere declaration" — "already but not yet," simul iustus et peccator. Reformed-adjacent, with the sacramental-realist accent. **[Stated, COT-4, COT-5, COT-6]**  
- **Eucharistic readings of the OT (relevant to the presence question):** the showbread, the Passover lamb (eaten), the grain/"meat" offering, and Melchizedek's bread-and-wine are all read as foreshadowing Holy Communion, in which "we receive the body and blood of Christ" — reinforcing his realist (not memorialist) presence view from a typological angle, while still denying re-sacrifice. **[Stated, COT-3, COT-5, COT-6, COT-7]**  
- **A datum about his circle (COT-6):** he mentions a "priest friend" who holds that the OT religion was a "paganized" capitulation to surrounding paganism — RJ explicitly *rejects* this (God Himself laid out the sacrificial system; it's not a concession to paganism). Tells you he's actively debating these questions with at least one other clergyman and stakes out the conservative side.  
- **[Analysis]** The hermeneutic is solidly within Reformed-covenantal bounds and is mostly *common ground* with you — which is itself useful: it means the incense tension (§13) and the presence question (§8) can be argued on *shared* typological premises rather than across a hermeneutical gulf. The one cross-tradition friction is the atonement-emphasis point (§5).

8. 🎯 **His own fulfillment hermeneutic vs. his incense/altar practice (COT-3/6 + Rev-9/10/11 vs AW-I) — the strongest internal lever in the corpus, now documented and sharpened.** The Revelation class makes the practice-rationale *explicit and repeated*: "this is one of the reasons we have incense… one of the reasons we have an altar… as the standard part of our worship" (Rev 8); "which is why we use incense at church" (Rev 5). So three sub-levers, in ascending force (full development in §13):  
   - **(a) Fulfillment (the original COT lever, restated to survive his escape hatch):** Hebrews forbids reviving fulfilled OT ceremonial because it implies Christ hasn't come; he typologizes the tabernacle altar of incense as a fulfilled shadow of "the prayers of the saints" and Christ's intercession — and Rev 8, on his own reading, *confirms* that the heavenly reality is intercession/prayer (the angel-priest plausibly Christ). So the vision points to the fulfilled reality; reproducing the shadow's physical apparatus is the move his Hebrews argument forbids.  
   - **(b) Apocalyptic genre (his own anti-literalist hermeneutic):** he reads the sea of glass, the living creatures, the 24 elders, the slain Lamb, the bowls as *symbols*, refusing to literalize them — so why is the incense the one piece of heavenly furniture reproduced as a literal physical object on earth? The selectivity is the tension.  
   - **(c) Signification:** he himself says the heavenly bowls/smoke *are* "the prayers of the saints" and that incense is "symbolic of prayer" — Revelation gives him the thing signified and the interpretation of the symbol, not a command to burn the physical signifier in the assembly.  
   - ⚠️ **The counter he will reach for — heavenly-pattern participation** ("our worship is reflecting their worship"; the Sursum Corda joins the angels' worship). This is a *real, respectable, non-OT-revival* warrant (and not uniquely EO — it has a thin Reformed form too: Calvin's *sursum corda*, Heb 12:22–24). **Do not deny it; locate the limit.** The Reformed grant participation in the heavenly *realities* but deny that a vision of heaven's symbolic *furniture* is a prescription to reproduce that furniture physically — the regulative authority for new-covenant elements is Christ's institution + apostolic practice (WCF 21.1), and Hebrews runs fulfillment *away from* re-materializing altars/incense. See §13 [Analysis — for JD] for the full Reformed handling of heavenly-pattern participation, including the Sanctus as the test case (we *sing* "holy holy holy" with the angels without installing a literal sea of glass).
