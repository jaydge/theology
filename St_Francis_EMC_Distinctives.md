## 2. Scripture & Authority

- **Affirms sola scriptura** via Article 6: Scripture "containeth all things necessary to salvation" — material sufficiency. **[Stated, A101-VI, III]**  
- Scripture is the **ultimate authority, infallible and inerrant**; everything measured against it; inerrancy allows for generalization/approximation. **[Stated, A101-V, VI]**  
- **Apocrypha:** mediating Article 6 position — read "for example of life and instruction of manners," never to establish doctrine. **[Stated, A101-VI]**  
- **Tradition does not replace or supersede Scripture**; it transmits Scripture and supplies interpretive context. Two "red flags" for any reading: (1) opposed by the earliest generations; (2) opposed by patristic consensus. Private opinion doesn't overrule consensus, but the fathers are a *check on interpretation*, not an independent doctrinal source. **[Stated, A101-VI]**  
- **Church not infallible** but has authority (Art. 20): may decree rites and settle controversies, revisable, never contrary to Scripture, never binding anything beyond Scripture for salvation. Keys (Matt 16/18) = church discipline, given to all apostles + successors, not Peter alone; Matt 16:18 = Peter's *confession* (Augustine, Ambrose, Athanasius). **[Stated, A101-V]**  
- **No total apostasy ever**: "we can reform, elucidate, contextualize — but never innovate" (Jude 3). The Spirit gives no new doctrine. **[Stated, A101-VI]**  
- ⚠️ **Operative-authority gap [Analysis]:** in *practice* his worship/ceremonial decisions run heavily on tradition — the Sarum Rite, the Fathers, "the historic church" — even where his stated principle is Scripture's sufficiency. Tradition functions as a near-coordinate authority in deed (filling space where Scripture is silent or, on his view, where a type is fulfilled), more than his stated doctrine of sola scriptura would suggest. This is the seam where the icons/ceremonial questions actually bite — see §11 and Open Questions.  
- **Doctrine of the fathers (ANF, RJ's own framing):** Christianity is "a received religion" — no generation without Christians (Matt 16:18); if we can't trust the fathers to transmit doctrine, we can't trust them to have transmitted the *Scriptures* they copied. The fathers are *closer to the apostles* (the great-great-grandfather analogy) and culturally fluent, so they're a "guide back to Christ and the apostles." But explicitly: **"not everything they say is true or even taught by all"** — flag idiosyncratic claims (Tertullian, Origen), and weigh especially the points of *universal consensus*. Where one's reading of Scripture cuts against a genuine patristic consensus, "be humble enough to be willing to submit our own interpretation." **[Stated, ANF-1]**  
  - **[Analysis]** This is a *high-consensus-Protestant* doctrine of tradition, not a Roman two-source one: the fathers check interpretation and carry heavy presumptive weight, but Scripture remains the only infallible norm and the fathers can err. It's close to (though slightly "higher" than) the Reformed *norma normata* view of the consensus patrum. The live tension is purely operative — see §3 and the Councils breakthrough.  
  - RJ's own most revealing question to a guest (ANF-4): *"What would you say to someone who reads Ignatius or Clement and says, 'I think he's a sincere Christian, but my reading of Scripture really goes against what he's saying'?"* — i.e., RJ himself names the sola-scriptura-vs-consensus problem as *the* major issue. He clearly feels its weight. **[Stated, ANF-4]**  
- **Sola scriptura in operation (Rev) — two confirming data points.** (1) **"No dogmatizing beyond warrant."** Teaching the four living creatures as the four Gospels (the Irenaeus/Jerome/Augustine tradition), RJ embraces it as *helpful tradition* — "it comes to us from tradition… we don't treat it on the level of scripture" — but explicitly refuses to bind it: *"we never want to dogmatize something that we don't have the warrant to dogmatize from scripture."* A clean, real-time instance of his stated *norma normata* posture: tradition illumines, Scripture norms, and he polices the line. (2) **Sola scriptura is not presuppositionless biblicism.** He explicitly warns against the "the only thing you look at is scripture and your cultural presuppositions magically wash away" caricature — "that's not how the reformers approached scripture" — and insists on reading each text in its genre and historical-cultural setting (exegesis, not isogesis), with the fathers as interpretive guides. A mature, defensible account of sola scriptura that a Reformed reader shares. **[Stated, Rev-3, Rev-6]**  
  - **[Analysis]** Both data points cut *for* RJ's stated principle and slightly *against* the operative-authority worry — on doctrine and interpretation he genuinely operates as a high-consensus Protestant (*norma normata*). This makes the operative-authority gap (above) look increasingly **localized to ceremonial/worship practice** rather than doctrine: he won't dogmatize the four-creatures reading without scriptural warrant, yet he *will* install literal incense and an altar on the strength of an apocalyptic vision he otherwise reads symbolically (§13). That asymmetry — rigorous about not over-reading Scripture for *doctrine*, looser about deriving *physical worship practice* from symbolic visions — is itself worth a gentle, admiring observation in conversation: it shows the seam is not "he's a crypto-Romanist on authority" but "his ceremonial outruns the hermeneutic he applies everywhere else."

## 3. Councils

- Website: "largely… the Seven Ecumenical Councils, with special emphasis on the first Four." **[Web]**  
- Positively invokes Councils 1–6 by name/content (Nicaea I, Constantinople I, Ephesus, Chalcedon, Constantinople II & III) for Trinitarian/Christological doctrine. **[Stated, A101-II, IV, AW-IV]**  
- 🎯 **BREAKTHROUGH (ANF-1):** RJ finally states his own position on Nicaea II. Asked whether councils are infallible, he says **no** — and adds, in his own words: *"certain ones like the Seventh Ecumenical Council, you're not required to… kiss icons or something. We wouldn't — we would say that's not a requirement, because that would force somebody into something they might not actually feel comfortable with and might feel like they're doing idolatry with it."* **[Stated, ANF-1]**  
  - **What this resolves:** he does **not** bind icon veneration on his people, and he explicitly grants that someone could reasonably regard it as idolatry. That is a substantially *Protestant* handling of Nicaea II and is consistent with Article 22 — it knocks out the strong "you must venerate" reading the trilemma feared.  
  - **What it leaves open:** he frames this as "not *required*" — which permits icon veneration as an option while not mandating it. He doesn't say it's *wrong*, only that it can't be *imposed*. He treats the Seventh Council the way he says Rome/EO treat canons in practice — selectively applied — rather than rejecting it outright. So the precise question shifts from "do you venerate?" to "**is veneration a legitimate option you'd defend, or merely one you won't impose?**" and "if the Seventh Council's anathemas against non-venerators are wrong, in what sense is it still 'ecumenical' for you?"  
- **Councils are not infallible** (his general view): he notes even Rome and EO hedge on conciliar infallibility in practice (popes move councils "in and out of authority"; EO bishops apply canons selectively), and says his own practical position is "virtually no different" — certain conciliar requirements simply aren't binding. **[Stated, ANF-1]**  
- **[Analysis]** This is the cleanest illustration yet of the operative-authority pattern (§2): Scripture and the first four/six councils are firm; the Seventh is held loosely and selectively, by *his* judgment of what may be imposed. That's a Protestant rule of faith in operation — and it's exactly the lever for the friendly version of question 1 (see Open Questions): not "you're too Catholic," but "by your own account you sit in private judgment over which conciliar canons bind — which is the very thing Rome says sola scriptura can't avoid."

## 4. Doctrine of God & Christology

- Classical theism: divine simplicity (no physical or metaphysical parts), immutability, impassibility, eternality (God outside time), one divine will. Rejects "theistic personalism" and the modern "three centers of will" model. Holds the Athanasian Creed essential against it; "no Anglican should reject classical theism." Reinforced at length in Misc-2025 (two near-identical Trinity Sunday sessions): rejects Sabellianism/modalism and Arianism, frames a modern "tritheism" as the live error (~last 200 yrs), ties everything to the one-divine-will test, uses Augustine's love-of-self/Father–Son–Spirit model, the sunbeam-on-wax-and-clay analogy for wrath=love, and the *univocal/equivocal/analogical* distinction (we speak of God only analogically). Roots it in Aristotle/Plato under Scripture's authority, defending the use of Greek philosophical terms (logos; "in him we live and move"). **[Stated, A101-II, AW-VI, Misc-2025]**  
  - One sharp parishioner objection worth noting (Misc-2025): a layman pushed back that the image-of-God→theistic-personalism inference "doesn't pass the giggle test" (the Mona Lisa is an image of a beautiful woman but isn't one). RJ agreed and used it to reinforce his rejection of theistic personalism. Tells you the congregation includes at least one philosophically sharp interlocutor and that RJ welcomes the pushback. **[Stated, Misc-2025]**  
- Chalcedonian, dyothelite Christology; God suffers/dies *according to the human nature*. **[Stated, A101-II, IV]**  
- Filioque: affirms the theology, sympathizes with the objection to its insertion. **[Stated, A101-II]**  
- Islam "100% a false religion"; not the same God; not "brothers and sisters." **[Stated, A101-IV]**

## 5. Soteriology

- **Justification by faith alone — affirmed explicitly and repeatedly.** Saved by Christ alone, grace through faith, not works. Law/gospel framework central to his preaching ("the law is the diagnosis… the gospel is the cure"); the law is good (a functional third use); Rom 6 against antinomianism. **[Stated, A101 II/III/VII, AW-I/IV]**  
- **Imputation; simul iustus et peccator** (from the Lutherans). **[Stated, A101-VIII]**  
- Election: affirms election to salvation; **rejects election to damnation** and supralapsarianism; personally leans conditional but holds either acceptable. **[Stated, A101-IV]**  
- **Rejects once-saved-always-saved**; apostasy possible. Repentance and faith are gifts of God. **[Stated, A101-VII]**  
- Rome: "you can find the gospel in the Roman Catholic church," but its justification doctrine/practice trends transactional. **[Stated, A101-IV]**  
- Rejects dispensationalism and the rapture. **[Stated, A101-II]**  
- **Eschatology (Rev): postmillennial + partial preterist.** Personally holds **postmillennialism** ("this is the one that I hold to") and **partial preterism** — most of Revelation already fulfilled by ~AD 70 (the destruction of the Jerusalem temple), though *not* the general resurrection. Optimistic "upward trajectory" view of church history. Explicitly does **not** dogmatize it: pre-/a-/post-millennial are all "within the realm of orthodoxy," and his jurisdiction has not made any one a confessional requirement. Explicitly rejects **hyper-preterism** as denying the creeds and the bodily resurrection. Reinforces and extends the anti-dispensationalism above. Detail in §18. **[Stated, Rev-1, Rev-7]**  
  - **[Analysis]** This is genuinely new and substantially **Reformed-friendly**. Postmillennialism has deep Puritan/Reformed and Anglican pedigree (he names Puritans, Presbyterians, and Anglicans as its post-Reformation carriers), and partial preterism is held across confessional-Reformed circles. Nothing here is a cross-tradition fault line with a PCA/OPC reader — at most an *intramural* eschatological difference (many in the PCA/OPC are amillennial; some are postmil). Pairs naturally with his covenant theology and one-people-of-God reading (§17). Worth noting as common ground, and as a useful tonal datum: he is irenic about millennial disagreement, which models the register he'll expect in conversation.  
- **Atonement (COT):** affirms penal/substitutionary atonement as "completely undeniable in scripture" — but warns against *reducing* the atonement to penal substitution alone, citing 1 Cor 15 (no forgiveness without the resurrection). Explicitly says he thinks **Christus Victor** (Christ's conquest of sin and death) may be "the most important" facet, "competitive with penal substitution," because it's the undoing of the ultimate enemy and most magnifies God's glory. Frames the atonement as multifaceted and the whole Incarnation (not just the cross) as redemptive. **[Stated, COT-4]**  
  - **[Analysis]** This is the one place his soteriology is audibly *not* standard confessional-Reformed. The Westminster tradition treats penal substitution as the central, organizing category of the atonement, with Christus Victor as a true but subordinate consequence of it. RJ inverts the emphasis. It's not heterodox — penal substitution is affirmed — but a PCA/OPC reader will hear the de-prioritizing of penal substitution as a meaningful divergence, and it's worth a gentle question about *ordering* (is Christ's victory the ground of, or the result of, the satisfaction of divine justice?). This is a genuine cross-tradition difference, not an internal-Anglican one.  
- **Dispensationalism (COT):** calls it "a very errant belief, I would say heretical" — two-peoples-of-God (church + ethnic Israel), with extreme forms allowing salvation apart from Christ; "flies in the face of Galatians." Strongly covenantal/one-people-of-God reading (Gentiles grafted into the same tree, Rom 11; heirs of Abraham via union with Christ, Gal 3). Reformed-friendly. **[Stated, COT-5]**

## 6. Sacraments — Number & Definition

- Augustine/Article 25 definition; sacraments are **God's promises, not our works** (engagement-ring analogy). **[Stated, A101-VII]**  
- **Two-tier, not flat seven:** two *dominical* (baptism, Holy Communion) instituted by Christ with forgiveness attached; five *ecclesial* (confirmation, penance/absolution, holy orders, matrimony, unction) sacraments "in a lesser sense" per the Homilies — the church may modify their signs but not their realities. **[Stated, A101-VII, VIII]**

## 7. Baptism

- **Baptismal regeneration affirmed strongly** — "I cannot think of a single early Church Father who denies" it; regeneration normatively tied to baptism, possible before/outside it. Unites to Christ's death (Rom 6); forgives sins past/present/future (quasi-atemporal); washing of regeneration; "baptism saves." Infant baptism affirmed; confirmation makes the faith one's own. Compatible with sola fide because baptism is God's promise received, not man's work; claims reformers held a *stronger* view than Rome's "second plank." **[Stated, A101-VI, VII, VIII]**  
- Holy-water stoup at the door: dip fingers, sign of the cross, "remember your baptism" (cites Luther). **[Stated, Recon-Euch]**  
- **Baptism + apostasy, refined (COT):** baptism "objectively" grafts onto Christ (Rom 6; 1 Pet 3:21 — the flood as the *figure*, baptism the greater reality). But he carefully distinguishes this from "losing your salvation": he says you *cannot* lose salvation, because apostasy is not accidental — it's "an intentional, full-throated… actual hatred of Christ" by which one throws away the ring (the engagement-ring/Steak-'n-Shake-thrown-ring image again). So a baptized person who apostatizes "has walked away from that regeneration." **[Stated, COT-2]**  
- **Apostasy restated (Rev) — confirms COT-2, same structure.** Teaching the Rom 11 olive tree (Rev 7 / the 144,000), RJ again distinguishes "stumbling/sinning" (which does *not* forfeit salvation) from "an active, willful, belligerent apostasizing" which is being "cut off" — and grounds it in Hebrews 6/10 and the warning passages, plus the 1 John 2:19 "they went out from us because they were not of us" category for those who only *seemed* to belong. He adds a careful anti-synergism guard on Phil 2:12–13 ("I don't want to make it sound synergistic… it is God who works in you"). No new movement from COT-2; it deepens and re-confirms the same FV-adjacent shape from a second class. **[Stated, Rev-5, Rev-8]**  
  - **[Analysis]** This is the cluster a PCA/OPC reader will scrutinize hardest: objective baptismal grafting + real (if deliberate) apostasy of the regenerate is exactly the conceptual neighborhood of **Federal Vision** (objective covenant membership conferred at baptism, forfeitable by covenant-breaking). RJ is *not* self-consciously FV and his vocabulary is more Lutheran/Prayer-Book than Wilkins/Wright, but the structure overlaps. His "you can't *lose* it, only *throw it away*" move is doing real work — it's how he reconciles baptismal regeneration with perseverance language. Worth probing how he relates this to the Reformed distinction between the visible and invisible church, and to WCF 17 on perseverance. **Note for framing the conversation:** my disagreement here is precisely about **baptismal regeneration and baptismal objectivity** (the FV-adjacent structure), *not* about the mode or proper subjects of baptism — I'm open to the credo-baptist (LBCF 1689) view of mode/subjects, so infant baptism per se isn't the fault line. Keep the pressure on regeneration-and-objectivity, not on paedo- vs. credo-baptism. This is the single most important place for the FV/NPP/justification paper to sharpen the final questions.

## 8. Eucharist — Presence

- **Real, substantial presence**; bread and wine *remain*. Reads John 6:52–58 eucharistically (with Calvin, against Luther). **[Stated, A101-VII, AW-V, Recon-Euch]**  
- **Two views both "acceptable" in Anglicanism, both in the formularies:** (a) Calvin/Vermigli — the faithful alone receive Christ; (b) Lutheran — the wicked also receive the body, to their condemnation. **RJ leans firmly Lutheran**: "I lean much more towards the Luther understanding." Notes Cranmer personally moved transubstantiation → Lutheran → Calvinist, "encompassing the debate as one person." **[Stated, AW-V, A101-VII]**  
- On the "body, blood, soul, divinity" phrase: RJ uses it to describe the **shared overlap** among Rome/Lutheran/Anglican/Reformed ("all of us would confess… Christ is truly present, his body, blood, soul, divinity are present"), then immediately **qualifies**: Christ is **not locally present** (cites Aquinas — moving a consecrated chalice doesn't move Christ), the differences are "philosophical," and "we should say… in receiving the bread and the wine we are receiving the body and blood of Christ." So the phrase is his summary of the catholic consensus he claims, not a Tridentine concomitance claim he is personally pressing. **[Stated, Recon-Euch — see Cross-Batch note 1]**  
- **Rejects transubstantiation** — not "damnable," but "overthrows the nature of a sacrament" (Art. 28) by destroying bread and wine. Some Anglicans hold it; he doesn't. **[Stated, A101-III/VII, AW-V]**  
- **Rejects mere memorialism** "pretty universally." **[Stated, A101-VII]**  
- Worthy reception: trinitarian baptism; no habitual unrepented sin; basic Nicene orthodoxy; belief in *some* form of real presence (softened pastorally — settled rejection is the problem, not weak faith). Open communion to baptized creedal Christians; rejected Eastern Orthodoxy in part over closed communion. **[Stated, A101-VII, I]**

## 9. Eucharist — Sacrifice

- **Rejects any re-sacrifice of Christ** (Heb; Art. 31); the cross is sufficient. The medieval "re-sacrifice" mindset (side-altar private masses) is the target. Notes 20th-c. Anglican–RC dialogue argues Rome no longer holds re-sacrifice; he'd "be very happy if that's the case" but the medieval on-the-ground understanding was real. **[Stated, AW-I/V, Recon-Euch]**  
- Affirms multiple *proper* senses of Eucharistic sacrifice (Perkins): praise/thanksgiving, alms, self-offering ("ourselves, our souls and bodies," Rom 12:1), commemoration; "Christ is rightly called the sacrifice insofar as He is present." **[Stated, AW-I/V]**  
- **Will not celebrate a private Mass**; no congregation → Morning Prayer instead. Canonically barred from private Mass in this jurisdiction. A Reformed-friendly instinct. **[Stated, AW-V, Recon-Euch]**

## 10. Eucharistic Ceremonial (lex orandi)

- Practices, in his own words: **elevation of the elements with bells** at consecration; **genuflection / solemn bow**; **reverencing ("kissing") the altar** (cites Jeremy Taylor); **sign of the cross with holy water** on entering; **reservation in a tabernacle** (explicitly "primarily for the sick"); **ablutions** of fingers and vessels; **mixing water with the wine** (practical + symbolic, water/blood from Christ's side); **prefers reception on the tongue** (does not require it); discourages self-intinction (hygiene). Treats "altar" and "table" as interchangeable. **[Stated, Recon-Euch]**  
- His **reverencing-the-altar reasoning**: explicitly *not* "bowing down and prostrating in an idolatrous way" but "respecting the sacred space," by analogy to reverence shown the Ark where God's presence was held — and he notes the OT was "almost iconoclastic in some senses." **[Stated, AW-IV — important nuance, see Cross-Batch note 2]**  
- Candles given a practical-plus-symbolic rationale (light pre-electricity; Christ light of the world; epistle/gospel candles). Vestments mostly practical/symbolic, repeatedly downplaying theological weight (the chasuble "because England gets cold"; the collar a Presbyterian/Church-of-Scotland invention). **[Stated, Recon-Euch]**  
- **His own tell:** "a lot of liturgy is the pragmatics of it being turned into theological reasons." **[Stated, Recon-Euch]**  
- **[Analysis]** Core tension worth probing gently: the *words* deny re-sacrifice and local presence; several *practices* (reservation, elevation, reverence directed at the consecrated elements) are exactly what Article 28's closing sentence lists as things the Sacrament was not given for ("reserved, carried about, lifted up, or worshipped"). But note his framing carefully — he frames reverence as toward sacred *space* and Christ's presence, not adoration of the host as object, and reservation as for the sick. The collision with Art. 28 is real but he has padding; this is a question, not a knockout.

## 11. Priesthood & Absolution

- Only the priest **pronounces** absolution (John 20:21–23); a "sacramental," exercised not in his own power but as God's commissioned official — the **IRS-agent analogy** (a stranger can't tell you your taxes are forgiven; an IRS official can). A layman may pray all of Morning/Evening Prayer **except the Absolution**. **[Stated, AW-III, Recon-Euch]**  
- **[Analysis]** The *declarative/ministerial* absolution is shared ground (the BCP absolution is itself declarative; the keys, Matt 16/18). Divergence is narrower than it looks: (1) restricting John 20:23 to an ordained order vs. the church exercising the keys through Word and discipline, and (2) the surrounding altar-sacrifice complex. Possible lever: BCP "priest" = *presbyteros* (presbyter), not *sacerdos*; the English Reformers leaned on the former.

## 12. Saints, Mary, Images, Icons, Statues — ⚠️ still mostly a gap, but new (smaller) data points

- **Veneration language IS present, but only generically and explicitly distinguished from worship:** "we look up to [heroes/great people of history] and we venerate them — we don't worship them, we don't give them that unique expression of adoration… we should only give to the Father." This is about *living heroes and historical figures* as an analogy for how worship conforms us, **not** a defense of venerating saints or images, and **not** invocation. The dulia/latria terms are NOT used. **[Stated, AW-I — see Cross-Batch note 3, which corrects the merge note]**  
- **"Icons" appears three times, all in passing**, always inside the "five senses / we are not brains on sticks" list (sight: "we see icons… vestments… the altar"). He never defends icon *veneration*, never addresses bowing/kissing/censing icons, never engages Nicaea II or Article 22. That the parish *has* icons is confirmed; their devotional use and his theology of it are **not**. **[Stated that they're present; Clarify on everything else]**  
- "The Blessed Virgin" appears once, purely descriptively (introducing the Magnificat at Evening Prayer). Notes some Puritans opposed the gospel canticles (Magnificat, Nunc Dimittis, Benedictus); Psalms were inserted as a compromise — descriptive history, no Marian doctrine asserted. **[Stated, AW-III]**  
- "Bowing at the name of Jesus" (simple bow) defended from Phil 2 / Rev — bowing to *Christ*, not to saints or images. **[Stated, AW-IV, Recon-Euch]**

## 13. Worship Theology & the Regulative Principle

- Worship is first our **duty** to God (He is worthy), not primarily for our benefit; by worship we are "conformed into the image of God." **[Stated, AW-I]**  
- Whole-person worship, all five senses; "we are not brains on sticks." Sharp critique of seeker-sensitive/entertainment worship (Jesus Movement, "decisions for Christ," emotional manipulation, church-shopping) — the irenic, self-aware "it's not about us, it's about him" anecdote. **[Stated, AW-I, Recon-Euch]**  
- **Regulative vs. normative principle:** concedes the terms are imprecise; argues Anglicans actually hold a *more consistent* regulative principle and that populist RPW adherents are inconsistent — incense (Exod 30; Mal 1:11; Rev 8), vestments (Exod 28; Rev 4), hymns beyond psalms (Matt 26:30; Eph 5:19), instruments (Ps 150), plus Jesus at Hanukkah and synagogue worship (neither OT-prescribed). On incense he concedes it "symbolizes the prayers" but defends the object: "a symbol doesn't work well if it's not there." **[Stated, AW-I, Recon-Euch]**  
- **[Analysis]** He refutes the *populist* RPW, not the confessional one. WCF 21.1 runs on command + approved example + good-and-necessary consequence; WCF 1.6 distinguishes **elements** (need warrant) from **circumstances** (time/place/posture, ordered by prudence). His examples tend to conflate the two or skip new-covenant fulfillment (Heb 7–10; Col 2:16–17). Incense arguably proves too much (Revelation's heavenly liturgy also has a literal altar, slain Lamb, sea of glass) — but the *external* "proves too much" point is now superseded by the *internal* levers below, which the Revelation class makes far stronger. Synagogue/Hanukkah are descriptive; the synagogue is itself a *circumstance*, illustrating rather than refuting the distinction.  
- 🎯 **STRONGEST NEW INTERNAL LEVER (COT-6 vs. AW-I) — his own fulfillment hermeneutic cuts against his incense argument.** In the Christ-in-the-OT class, RJ argues *at length* that reviving OT ceremonial is wrong precisely because those rites *pointed forward* to a Christ who had not yet come, and are now *fulfilled*: "the epistle to the Hebrews warns against going back to the ways of the old covenant… now its purpose is fulfilled." He says our liturgy must point to the *present, risen* Christ, and that re-adopting uniquely-OT practices amounts to "implicitly, liturgically, ceremonially saying that this [Christ's coming] has not yet happened." **[Stated, COT-3, COT-6]**  
  - **This is the Reformed fulfillment argument against incense, in his own mouth.** His Worship-series defense of incense leans on Malachi 1:11 and the Revelation/heavenly-temple imagery — i.e., on OT-and-apocalyptic *ceremonial* warrant. But by his own COT principle, OT incense (Exod 30; the altar of incense in the Tabernacle, which he treats typologically as pointing to "the prayers of the saints" and ultimately to Christ) is a *shadow fulfilled in Christ* — so reviving it as literal gathered-worship practice is exactly the move he condemns. He even explicitly typologizes the tabernacle incense as fulfilled. The friendly question writes itself: *"You taught that Hebrews forbids reviving fulfilled OT ceremonial because it implies Christ hasn't come — how is burning literal incense, an OT tabernacle rite you yourself read as a fulfilled type, not subject to your own rule?"* This is far stronger than the external "incense proves too much" critique because it's **his own stated hermeneutic turned on his own practice** — an internal-consistency question, not a cross-tradition one.  
- 🎯🎯 **THE REVELATION CLASS NAILS THE HINGE — and complicates it (Rev-9, Rev-10, Rev-11; full detail in §18).** The 2025 Revelation class moves the incense/altar warrant from *inference* to *flat, repeated, in-his-own-voice statement*. On the golden censer and altar of Rev 8:3–5 he says: *"not only is incense being used, but an altar as well. **Again, we take a lot of our worship theology from the book of Revelation. This is one of the reasons we have incense. This is one of the reasons why we have an altar.**"* Then, stacking Exod 30 + Luke 1 + Malachi 1:11: *"a central aspect of worship, both the incense and the altar. **And that's how it is for us. We have both as part of our worship, as the standard part of our worship.**"* On the Rev 5:8 bowls of incense: *"Psalm 141:2… we see that all throughout the Old Testament, **which is why we use incense at church.**"* So the practice's stated rationale is now explicit and threefold: (a) OT prescription (Exod 30), (b) OT prophecy of NT incense (Mal 1:11), and (c) the heavenly liturgy of Revelation. This **reinforces** OQ8 decisively on the OT-revival horn — (a) and (b) are precisely the fulfilled shadows his COT hermeneutic says we must not revive.  
  - **But the Revelation class also hands him his escape hatch, and you need to see it clearly before pressing.** Threaded through the class is a *governing principle* he states outright: **earthly worship participates in / patterns itself on heavenly worship.** On the Sanctus of Rev 4:8 → the Sursum Corda: *"we are joining in the heavenly worship with the angels and archangels… we get that from Revelation 4."* On Rev 5: *"this isn't reflecting our worship. **Our worship is reflecting their worship.**"* On Rev 7: *"our worldview should be first and foremost informed by our worship."* Call this the **heavenly-pattern-participation** warrant. It is *not* the OT-revival argument, and it is not obviously subject to the COT fulfillment rule, because the worship of Rev 4–5 is presented as the *present, ongoing, post-ascension* reality of heaven — not a fulfilled-and-retired OT shadow. So if you press only the COT-vs-incense lever, he will answer: *"I'm not reviving Exodus 30; I'm joining the worship of heaven that John saw, which is happening now."* That answer neutralizes the simplest form of the question.  
  - **The sharpened question (what OQ8 now has to do).** The lever still holds, but it has to be aimed at the *seam between his two warrants*, not just the OT one. Three moves, in ascending order of force:  
    1. **The signification problem.** RJ himself says the heavenly incense *is* a symbol — Rev 5:8 and 8:3–4 *interpret* the bowls/smoke as "the prayers of the saints," and he affirms "incense is all throughout scripture symbolic of prayer." So Revelation gives him the *thing signified* (the prayers of the saints ascending) and the *interpretation of the symbol* — it does not obviously command the *physical signifier* (literal burning incense) in the earthly assembly. The heavenly altar in Rev 8 has no human celebrant lighting a thurible; the censer is in an angel's (or Christ's) hand. By what warrant does "heaven displays the reality that prayer ascends like incense" license "therefore we burn the physical substance"? This is the cleanest pressure point and it is *internal*: he reads the bowls symbolically, then materializes the symbol.  
    2. **The apocalyptic-genre problem (uses his own hermeneutic again).** He insists, correctly and repeatedly, that Revelation's imagery is *not* "woodenly literal" — the sea of glass, the slain Lamb with seven horns and seven eyes, the 24 elders, the living creatures, the bowls, the golden altar are all symbolic/typological, read through their scriptural interpretation. He will not build a literal sea of glass or seat 24 literal elders. So *why is the incense the one element of the heavenly furniture that gets reproduced as a literal physical object on earth,* when every other item in the same vision he treats as symbol pointing to a reality? The selectivity is the tension: a consistent application of his own anti-literalist apocalyptic hermeneutic would read the heavenly incense as it reads the heavenly sea — as a symbol of a reality (prayer, peace) the church *has in Christ*, not as a blueprint for sanctuary furniture.  
    3. **The fulfillment problem, restated to survive the escape hatch.** Even granting heavenly-pattern participation, his COT rule was about *which* OT shadows are fulfilled-and-retired. He typologizes the tabernacle altar of incense as a shadow of "the prayers of the saints" and ultimately of Christ's intercession (Heb 7–10; cf. §17, where Christ "daily presents himself before the Father interceding"). Revelation 8 *confirms* that typology — the heavenly reality is intercession/prayer, mediated by the angel-priest who is plausibly Christ (RJ's own read). So the heavenly scene, on his interpretation, points to *Christ's heavenly intercession and the saints' prayers* — exactly the fulfilled reality Hebrews says replaces the shadow. Reviving the *shadow's physical apparatus* (altar + incense) in the earthly assembly, when the vision itself directs us to the *fulfilled reality*, is the move his Hebrews argument forbids — now with Revelation as a witness *for* the fulfillment reading rather than against it.  
  - **[Analysis — for JD]** "Heavenly-pattern participation" is a real and respectable category, and it is *not* uniquely Eastern Orthodox, though the EO press it hardest (the Divine Liturgy as participation in the heavenly liturgy; the iconostasis as the heaven-earth boundary). It also has Roman, Lutheran, and high-Anglican forms, and there is a *thin* version even the Reformed grant. **How the classically Reformed tradition handles it** (so you can engage it without either conceding it or attacking a legitimate idea):  
    - *The Reformed do affirm a real participation.* Calvin's eucharistic doctrine is built on *sursum corda* — by the Spirit the worshipper is *lifted up to heaven* to feed on the ascended Christ, rather than Christ being brought down to the elements (*Institutes* 4.17.31–36). WCF 21 and the Heidelberg Catechism assume the gathered church joins the worship of the church triumphant. Hebrews 12:22–24 ("you have come to Mount Zion… to the assembly of the firstborn… to innumerable angels") is the Reformed proof-text that earthly worship *already* participates in the heavenly. So the Reformed answer to RJ is **not** "there is no heavenly participation" — that would be conceding the field on a point Scripture teaches. The disagreement is about **what participation licenses.**  
    - *The Reformed limit is the same one that governs the whole RPW: participation transmits the heavenly* ***realities*** *(access to God, communion with Christ and the saints, the prayers ascending, the "holy holy holy"), not a mandate to* ***reproduce the heavenly furniture as physical objects.*** The Reformed read Hebrews as teaching that the *true* tabernacle is heaven itself (Heb 8:1–5, 9:11–24) and that the earthly copies were shadows now fulfilled — so the direction of fulfillment runs *away from* re-materializing altars and incense, not *toward* it. To participate in the heavenly worship is to do by faith and the Spirit what heaven does in reality; it is *not* to build a scale model of heaven's sanctuary. The Sanctus is the test case the Reformed happily accept: we *sing* the "holy, holy, holy" with the angels (a reality-participation in the praise) — we do not therefore install a literal sea of glass, six-winged creatures, or 24 thrones. Incense is the item where RJ crosses from participating-in-the-reality to reproducing-the-furniture, and that crossing is exactly what the genre (symbol, not blueprint) and the fulfillment hermeneutic (shadow→reality, not reality→new-shadow) both forbid.  
    - *The element/circumstance distinction does the same work from the other side.* Even if heaven "uses incense," heaven's worship is not the *regulative authority* for the elements of new-covenant gathered worship — Christ's institution and apostolic practice are (WCF 21.1). The NT descriptions of gathered worship (Acts 2:42; 1 Cor 11–14; Col 3:16; Eph 5:19; 1 Tim 2) prescribe Word, prayer, sacrament, psalmody — and contain *no* instituted incense, altar-sacrifice, or thurifer for the assembly. A vision of heaven is not a church order. (This is the point where his "Jesus at the synagogue / Hanukkah" examples also fail: descriptive, not prescriptive; and the synagogue is itself a circumstance.)  
    - **Net for the conversation:** don't deny heavenly-pattern participation — affirm it warmly (Heb 12:22–24; Calvin's *sursum corda*), then locate the disagreement precisely: *participation in the heavenly reality is granted; the question is whether the vision of heaven's symbolic furniture is a* ***prescription to reproduce that furniture physically*** *in the earthly assembly — and on your own apocalyptic hermeneutic (symbol, not camcorder) and your own Hebrews/COT fulfillment hermeneutic (shadow→fulfilled reality), it isn't.* That keeps you from attacking something legitimate while still landing the internal-consistency point. This is the single strongest line in the corpus and the Revelation class both sharpened it and told you exactly which counter he'll reach for.  
    - **Sources to pull from [Analysis, added v0.8]:** the keystone is **John Owen, *An Exposition of the Epistle to the Hebrews* (chs. 8–10)** — the new-covenant priest, sacrifice, tabernacle, and altar are all *heavenly* (Christ's), and the heavenly realities could not be introduced without the *removal* of the earthly types: the precise answer to "heaven has an altar, so we have one." Pair with **Calvin, *Institutes* 4.17.31–36** (the *sursum corda*) + **Heb 12:22–24** for the participation granted, and **Calvin on Col 2:16–17** for the shadow→substance direction; codified in **WCF 21.1** and the **Westminster Directory (1645)**. The closest *historical* precedent is **intra-Anglican**: the Laudian "altar wars" of the 1630s, where the avant-garde (Andrewes, Cosin, Laud) reproduced heaven's throne-room in the chancel — at Durham, Cosin's gilded star-painted ceiling and ranks of carved angels — on a "beauty of holiness / gateway to heaven" rationale, and conforming Reformed Anglicans objected (**Peter Smart**, Durham, 7 July 1628, deprived and imprisoned; **Prynne** and **Burton** vs. Cosin's *Collection of Private Devotions*). Authoritative history: **Kenneth Fincham & Nicholas Tyacke, *Altars Restored* (OUP, 2007)**. And RJ's Revelation-as-liturgy reading has a recognizable modern provenance: **Scott Hahn, *The Lamb's Supper: The Mass as Heaven on Earth* (1999)**, echoing John Paul II's "mysterious participation in the heavenly liturgy" — a *Roman* line, not an Anglican-formulary one, worth engaging as the steel-man. **Cross-ref §12:** the bronze-serpent datum (incense — *qiṭṭēr* — burned to a God-commanded object, condemned and the object destroyed: 2 Kings 18:4) links the incense lever here to the images question in §12.

## 17. Christ in the OT Class — Hermeneutic & Covenant Theology (2025, all RJ)

- **Method: christotelic typology.** The OT is read as a unified story pointing forward to Christ — shadow→substance (Moses *foreshadows*, Christ *casts the shadow*); the whole arc is "the undoing of the fall," Genesis 3 → Revelation 22; redemption = the rewinding of fall/flood/Babel. Heavy on *fulfillment* language (37×) and *type/shadow*. Reads explicitly Christological types throughout: the protoevangelium (Gen 3:15), the flood-as-baptism (1 Pet 3:21, baptism the *greater* reality), Passover lamb/blood-on-doorposts → cross + Eucharist, the bronze serpent (John 3:14), Melchizedek's bread-and-wine → Eucharist (Heb 7), Moses as prophet-priest-king → Christ, Joshua (=Yeshua) leading into the land, David as shepherd/warrior/psalmist/king → Christ. **[Stated, COT-1 through COT-7]**  
- **Covenant theology, one people of God:** Gentiles grafted into the same tree (Rom 11); heirs of Abraham's promises via union with Christ the true "seed" (Gal 3); promised land → all creation in the resurrection. This is the engine of his anti-dispensationalism. **[Stated, COT-5]**  
- **Creation:** *creatio ex nihilo* defended against "God merely formed pre-existing matter"; ties it to classical theism and divine aseity. Genesis 1:26 read as a hint of the Trinity. **[Stated, COT-1]**  
- **Law/gospel + union with Christ:** the OT sacrificial system instills "blood atonement is necessary for forgiveness"; Christ the final, non-repeatable sacrifice (Heb), who "daily presents himself before the Father interceding" (note: this intercession-language recurs and is doing the same work as his Eucharistic "re-presentation, not re-sacrifice" line — §9/§10). Salvation = union with Christ, received in *word and sacrament*, not "mere declaration" — "already but not yet," simul iustus et peccator. Reformed-adjacent, with the sacramental-realist accent. **[Stated, COT-4, COT-5, COT-6]**  
- **Eucharistic readings of the OT (relevant to the presence question):** the showbread, the Passover lamb (eaten), the grain/"meat" offering, and Melchizedek's bread-and-wine are all read as foreshadowing Holy Communion, in which "we receive the body and blood of Christ" — reinforcing his realist (not memorialist) presence view from a typological angle, while still denying re-sacrifice. **[Stated, COT-3, COT-5, COT-6, COT-7]**  
- **A datum about his circle (COT-6):** he mentions a "priest friend" who holds that the OT religion was a "paganized" capitulation to surrounding paganism — RJ explicitly *rejects* this (God Himself laid out the sacrificial system; it's not a concession to paganism). Tells you he's actively debating these questions with at least one other clergyman and stakes out the conservative side.  
- **[Analysis]** The hermeneutic is solidly within Reformed-covenantal bounds and is mostly *common ground* with you — which is itself useful: it means the incense tension (§13) and the presence question (§8) can be argued on *shared* typological premises rather than across a hermeneutical gulf. The one cross-tradition friction is the atonement-emphasis point (§5).
