# Incense in Worship: A Reasoning Outline for Peer Review

**Purpose:** This outlines the case against burning incense as an act of worship in the Christian service, and also against treating it as a mere circumstance of worship. It is built to function in short conversations where every claim must survive scrutiny on its own. The intended audience for this draft is a pastor reviewer assessing the soundness of the reasoning. The context is a dialogue with an Anglo-Catholic Anglican minister who defends the use of incense primarily on the ground that Malachi 1:11 names it as a standard element of new covenant worship, and secondarily on the ground that Revelation 5:8 and 8:3-5 describe heaven's worship including incense, which the earthly church participates in and reflects.
 
---
 
## The two-minute spoken core
 
"The defenders of incense in worship don't treat it as a mere circumstance, and they're right not to: they ground it in Malachi 1:11 as 'a standard part of the worship of the people of God in the New Testament church,' and press whether a church without it is violating that verse. That makes it a claimed act of worship on divine warrant, and the only question is whether the warrant holds. The warrant offered comes in two versions that can't both be true. Either incense never stopped, in which case the incense law never stopped either, and that law made incense outside the sanctuary, by non-priests, with a non-prescribed compound, a capital offense. Or Malachi institutes something new for the new covenant church, in which case one prophetic verse, in Old Testament sacrificial vocabulary, with a grain offering in the same breath that nobody performs, is carrying the whole weight, against the New Testament's own reading that the incense is the prayers of the saints. Which we do offer. In every place."
 
---
 
## The full outline
 
### Step 1\. Begin from the pro-incense position itself
 
The pro-incense case, as typically made in Anglo-Catholic contexts, does not defend incense as a neutral circumstance or a piece of decoration. It defends it as a Godward act with positive divine warrant: Malachi 1:11 ("from the rising of the sun even unto the going down of the same my name shall be great among the Gentiles; and in every place incense shall be offered unto my name, and a pure offering") is read as naming incense as a standard element of new covenant worship, such that a church without it may be in violation of the verse.
 
Starting from this claim is stronger than opening with a denial of something they may not have said. If their own case establishes incense as an act of worship on divine warrant, the classification is settled by their account, and the only live question is whether the warrant holds.
 
### Step 2\. The three-bucket framework (for context, and for third parties who reach for weaker defenses)
 
Reformed worship theology distinguishes three categories:
 
- **Elements:** acts by which the congregation approaches God, requiring positive warrant (a command, apostolic example, or necessary inference from Scripture). Examples: reading, preaching, prayer, singing, baptism, the Supper.  
- **Circumstances:** unavoidable modal conditions of any commanded act, such as time, place, and seating. These require common sense, not a proof text.  
- **Adornment:** things indifferent to worship and carrying no Godward significance, such as flowers or paint color.
Incense cannot be a circumstance, because no commanded act requires it in order to happen; the service is whole without it. It cannot be adornment, because its defenders present it as a Godward offering with imposed sacred meaning. By their own description it lands in the element category, which requires positive warrant.
 
This step is mainly defensive: if a third party tries to rescue incense as a mere circumstance or adiaphoron, the framework answers it. The pro-incense case already concedes the element classification by the way it defends the practice.
 
### Step 3\. The warrant fork
 
Two warrants are offered for incense, and they are in tension with each other.
 
**(a) Continuity: "non-bloody ceremonial was never rescinded."**
 
The claim is that the fulfillment accomplished by Christ covers only the bloody sacrificial system, and that non-bloody ceremonial, including incense, continues as a valid form of worship in the new covenant church.
 
This warrant is caught by a three-part framework:
 
1. An unrescinded incense law includes Exodus 30 whole: sanctuary only (Exod 30:1-9), Aaronic priests only (Num 16; 2 Chron 26:16-21), the prescribed compound only, with imitation explicitly forbidden on pain of being cut off (Exod 30:37-38), and strange fire on pain of death (Lev 10:1-2). The practice as performed keeps none of these: wrong place, wrong priesthood, and (almost certainly) a non-prescribed commercial compound. If the law was never rescinded, it condemns the practice as performed.  
     
2. If the restrictions were lifted in Christ, the only scriptural mechanism offered is Hebrews 7:12: "the priesthood being changed, there is made of necessity a change also of the law." Invoking that verse to lift the restrictions concedes that the change of priesthood reached the incense law, which is the fulfillment argument itself. The continuity defense and the escape from Exodus 30's restrictions cannot both be true.  
     
3. Leviticus 10 is commonly cited by advocates of regulated worship as the hallmark passage showing that God cares about the manner of incense, not just its presence (Nadab and Abihu offered real incense, Godward, and died for the unauthorized manner). The question the manner-matters principle raises is where the manner of current incense practice is prescribed, since the texts offered as warrant specify nothing about substance, officiant, or occasion.
**(b) New institution: "Malachi 1:11 commands incense for the new covenant church."**
 
The claim is that Malachi's prophecy of universal, pure worship constitutes a positive institution of incense as a new covenant ordinance, such that churches that omit it are in violation of the verse.
 
Four problems:
 
1. **Wrong vocabulary for a new institution.** The verse uses the technical Levitical cult terms of its hearers: muqtar (that which is burned), muggash (offered), minchah (grain offering), offered "to my name." What the verse marks as new is scope ("in every place, among the nations") and quality ("pure"), not a new ordinance. Prophets routinely describe future realities in the sacrificial vocabulary of their day; institution language (command, appointment, prescribed manner) is absent.  
     
2. **No New Testament confirmation.** Every new covenant ordinance has a New Testament institution: baptism (Matt 28:19), the Supper (1 Cor 11:23-26), the Lord's Day (apostolic example, Acts 20:7; 1 Cor 16:2). A Malachi-instituted incense rite would be the only ordinance resting on a single prophetic verse with no dominical command, no apostolic example, and no necessary inference from any New Testament passage. The New Testament uses incense imagery repeatedly and consistently renders it as prayer and praise: Revelation 5:8 names the incense as the prayers of the saints; Hebrews 13:15 names the sacrifice of praise as the offering; Romans 15:16 echoes Malachi's offering-of-the-nations frame and describes Paul's priestly service as presenting the Gentiles as an offering. First Timothy 2:8 ("I will therefore that men pray every where, lifting up holy hands") even carries Malachi's "in every place" and applies it to prayer, not smoke. The apostolic interpretation of the verse is settled and unanimous.  
     
3. **No specified content.** A genuine institution regulates its own act: it specifies what the act is, who performs it, with what materials, and how. Exodus 30 does this for the Levitical incense at length. Malachi 1:11 specifies nothing: no substance, no officiant, no occasion, no manner. Every concrete feature of the actual practice (a thurible on chains, censing the altar and the people and the gifts, at particular liturgical moments, with particular compounds) comes from post-apostolic ecclesiastical tradition, not from the verse. This means tradition, not Malachi, is the operative warrant, with the verse recruited as corroboration afterward.  
     
4. **Read literally, the verse cannot even be permission.** "In every place, among the nations" describes worship that, under the Torah that the continuity warrant claims survives, cannot possibly be literal Levitical incense: non-Aaronic hands, outside the sanctuary, is the definition of strange fire on pain of death. The verse only describes lawful universal worship if its incense is figurative. Revelation 5:8 supplies the figure: the prayers of the saints. First Timothy 2:8 supplies the fulfillment: prayer in every place.
**(c) The fork as a direct question.**
 
"**If incense never stopped, why does it need Malachi to command it? If Malachi institutes it fresh, why insist it was never rescinded?**" The two warrants cannot run simultaneously. The continuity argument says the fulfillment line spared incense; the Malachi argument says incense needed a prophetic re-issue for the gospel age. Each horn of the fork has a prepared answer, and the fork itself exposes the underlying instability.
 
### Step 4\. The minchah parallel (against "the pure offering is just the incense")
 
A common response to the clause-mate problem is to read "a pure offering" (minchah) as epexegetical, restating the incense rather than naming a second item. Both readings cut the same way:
 
- **If two items:** minchah is the technical term for the grain offering of Leviticus 2, a bloodless altar offering. The same hermeneutic that reads incense literally must read the grain offering literally, and nobody performs a grain offering. Spiritualizing the second member of the clause concedes the hermeneutic that spiritualizes the first. Synonymous parallelism in one clause cannot be read half literal and half figural.  
- **If one item (the pure offering is the incense):** then incense is itself classified as a minchah, placing it inside the sacrificial offering category. That catches it in the fulfillment of the offering system and collapses the bloodless-therefore-surviving distinction, since the minchah was bloodless and unambiguously part of the altar cult.
Context reinforces both readings: the whole dispute of Malachi 1 is about defiled offerings on the altar, "the table of the LORD" (vv. 7-8, 12-13). The "pure offering" of v. 11 is the contrast to those altar offerings, tracking the same category.
 
### Step 5\. The fulfillment frame
 
The fulfillment line Scripture draws is shadow versus substance (Col 2:16-17; Heb 10:1), not bloody versus non-bloody. This is important because the most common objection to the fulfillment argument is that only the bloody sacrificial system was fulfilled in Christ, while non-bloody ceremonial continues.
 
Several responses:
 
1. The bloody/non-bloody distinction is the claim under dispute; invoking it as the rebuttal is circular. The question is whether Scripture actually draws that line, and it needs to be established from the texts, not assumed.  
     
2. Scripture's own worked examples of "shadow" are predominantly non-bloody. Colossians 2:16-17 applies "a shadow of things to come" to food, drink, festivals, new moons, and sabbaths, none of which is a blood rite. Hebrews 9:2-4 lists the tabernacle furniture Christ's entry fulfilled, including the lampstand, the table and bread of the Presence, and the golden censer and incense altar, all bloodless. The bloodless/surviving distinction is not only unproven; the texts' own extension of the shadow category contradicts it. A third witness is the grain offering itself: bloodless, unambiguously part of the altar cult, and claimed by no one to survive.  
     
3. Hebrews 9:4 specifically places the incense apparatus inside the first-covenant furniture declared fulfilled. Revelation 5:8 names its meaning: the prayers of the saints. Every link in this chain is a citation, not a construal. Against "you're misinterpreting Colossians 2": the minimal reading is uncontested (Paul calls the Mosaic ceremonial observances shadows whose body is Christ). The only interpretive step added is asking whether tabernacle incense is inside the category "shadow," and Hebrews 9:4 answers it by name.
### Step 5b\. What the incense actually symbolized, and the question chain it yields
 
The Old Testament gives the symbol two connected layers, and both have named fulfillments.
 
The daily incense signified prayer ascending. It burned on the golden altar morning and evening at the hours of the daily offerings (Exod 30:7-8), and Scripture supplies the meaning from inside the canon: "Let my prayer be set forth before thee as incense; and the lifting up of my hands as the evening sacrifice" (Ps 141:2). Luke 1:10 shows the association in practice: while Zacharias offered the incense within, the people prayed outside at the hour of incense. Note the mediation built into the rite: the people prayed outside, the priest carried the symbol inside. It did not merely say "prayer rises"; it said "prayer rises through the appointed mediator."
 
The Day of Atonement incense signified covering for a mediator entering the Presence. Aaron takes coals and incense within the veil "that the cloud of the incense may cover the mercy seat that is upon the testimony, that he die not" (Lev 16:12-13). The weightiest incense act in the system symbolizes the very problem the system existed to address: that sinful men, even the high priest, even once a year, even with blood, could not stand before God unshielded. Numbers 16:46-48 adds the intercessory function, Aaron standing with the censer between the dead and the living.
 
So the type is specific: the prayers of God's people, ascending through an appointed priestly mediator, made acceptable and safe in God's presence. Both halves have named antitypes. The prayers: Revelation 5:8 ("golden vials full of odours, which are the prayers of saints"), with 8:3-4 showing the mediation in visionary form. The mediator: Hebrews, where Christ has entered within the veil for us (6:19-20; 9:24) and ever lives to make intercession (7:25), which is why the church may come boldly to the throne (4:16) rather than standing outside at the hour of incense. The intercession the smoke screened is now performed, continually, by the living Christ. The type has no remainder; both of its referents are present realities.
 
That yields a simple chain of questions:
 
1. What did the incense symbolize in the Old Testament? Prayer ascending to God, through a priestly mediator whose intercession made the approach safe.  
     
2. What does the incense represent in the vision in Revelation? The prayers of the saints (Rev 5:8), presented with heavenly mediation (8:3-4). Same symbol, same referents, stated by the text itself.  
     
3. How does a New Testament Christian offer that incense? By praying (1 Tim 2:8; Heb 13:15), through the one mediator whose intercession is no longer symbolized but actual (Heb 7:25; 4:16).  
     
4. Then what is being offered, or symbolized, when incense is burned in a New Testament service? If it symbolizes the congregation's prayers, those prayers are already being offered in the same service, as the reality; why re-present them in shadow? And if it symbolizes the mediation, that work is Christ's, presently performed; what does a minister's censer add to it?
The second half of question 4 is the sharper one in a high-church setting. Censing performed by a vested minister at an altar does not read as "the congregation's prayers"; it reads as priestly mediation, and priestly mediation is the one thing the New Testament says is now exclusively Christ's (1 Tim 2:5; Heb 7:23-25).
 
The likeliest reply is that the incense accompanies the prayer as its visible sign. But a God-given sign accompanied the reality under the old covenant precisely because the reality was mediated at a distance: people outside, priest inside, veil between. The new covenant's change is the removal of that distance (Heb 10:19-22). Reinstating the sign re-stages the distance the gospel abolished. And the accompaniment claim faces the warrant question in miniature: the Supper accompanies the gospel with a visible sign because Christ instituted the accompaniment. Who instituted this one?
 
This also answers the demand to show where the New Testament revokes incense. It never has to name it. The incense was the ritual dress of priestly intercession at the point of entry into the Presence, and the argument of Hebrews 7-10 is that the entry has been accomplished and the intercession is live. Abolish the mediating priesthood's approach-work and the smoke that clothed it has nothing left to clothe. The New Testament does not revoke the costume separately; it announces that the actor has finished the scene.
 
### Step 5c\. Prophetic sacrificial vocabulary: the consistency test
 
The Malachi argument depends on reading one prophetic verse's cultic vocabulary literally. The prophets routinely describe the worship of the coming age in the Levitical vocabulary of their hearers, and in every parallel case the church reads the vocabulary as figure. A partial list:
 
- **Malachi 3:3-4**, two chapters later: purified "sons of Levi" offering a minchah "in righteousness," pleasant to the LORD "as in the days of old." Nobody restores a literal Levitical order. The purification belongs to the "day of his coming" (3:1-2), which Jesus applies to John the Baptist (Matt 11:10; Luke 7:27), so the horizon is Christ's coming. The hermeneutical switch would have to happen inside one prophet.  
- **Zechariah 14:16-21**: the nations go up to Jerusalem yearly to keep the Feast of Tabernacles, on pain of drought, and every pot in Jerusalem becomes holy for sacrificing. Universal Gentile worship, the same theme as Malachi 1:11, cast as pilgrimage feast and sacrifice. No one requires Booths of the church, and the holy cookware is read as figure by everyone.  
- **Ezekiel 40-48**: a full temple with dimensions, a functioning altar, sin and burnt offerings, a Zadokite priesthood, a prince offering sacrifices. Read literally it prescribes restored animal sacrifice in the age to come. Revelation reworks its imagery for a New Jerusalem with no temple at all (Rev 21:22).  
- **Isaiah 19:19-21**: an altar to the LORD in Egypt, with sacrifice and oblation (minchah), in the day of salvation. Read literally, it prescribes what Deuteronomy 12 forbids.  
- **Isaiah 56:6-7**: foreigners' burnt offerings accepted on the altar, in the house of prayer for all nations. Jesus quotes the prayer clause as the verse's point (Mark 11:17); no one takes the burnt-offerings clause as standing orders. One verse whose sacrificial half everyone spiritualizes and whose prayer half everyone keeps.  
- **Isaiah 66:20-21**: the nations brought as "an offering [minchah] unto the LORD," and from them God takes "priests and Levites." Paul's own commentary applies this vocabulary to his ministry: "the offering up of the Gentiles" (Rom 15:16). People, not grain, are the offering.  
- **Jeremiah 33:17-18**: a perpetual Levitical ministry offering burnt offerings and kindling grain offerings continually, in the same oracle as the perpetual Davidic throne. Christians read both halves as fulfilled in Christ's kingship and priesthood. A perpetuity formula attached to sacrifices everyone agrees have ended is a direct answer to "it was never rescinded" reasoning.
And Scripture supplies its own key to the idiom. Hosea 14:2 calls praise "the calves of our lips," and Hebrews 13:15 picks up the phrase to name the church's sacrifice as "the fruit of our lips." Psalm 141:2 makes incense stand for prayer inside the Psalter, centuries before Revelation 5:8 says the same thing.
 
The point is not that prophecy never means what it says. It is that a hermeneutic must be consistent. Every tradition, including the Anglo-Catholic, reads Ezekiel's altar, Zechariah's Booths, Isaiah's Egyptian altar, and Jeremiah's perpetual Levites as figures of the worship of the age to come. Malachi's incense becomes the sole exception selected for literalism, and the selection tracks a practice needing a warrant rather than any feature of the text.
 
One anticipated reply is that Ezekiel and Zechariah are eschatological while Malachi describes the present church age. But the defense of incense treats Malachi 1:11 as describing the church's worship now, and Zechariah 14 and Isaiah 19 are likewise "in that day" oracles of the nations' ingathering, which on that same reading is now. The distinction needs independent grounding.
 
A note on Isaiah 56, since it invites a first-century reading: the oracle cannot have been fulfilled by the pre-70 temple. Proselyte access already existed under the Torah (Exod 12:48), so the oracle would promise nothing new; it also welcomes the eunuch (56:3-5), whom Deuteronomy 23:1 excluded outright with no proselyte path, and the first eunuch brought in is Acts 8, by baptism. The Herodian temple ran the opposite direction, confining Gentiles behind a barrier that warned foreigners of death for passing further (cf. Acts 21:28-29). And Jesus cites the verse against that temple, not as fulfilled by it: the commerce he overturned occupied the court of the Gentiles, the one space the nations had. When the nations did stream in, the apostles declined to route them through the sacrificial system at all (Acts 15), which is the clearest possible commentary on what "their burnt offerings accepted on mine altar" was understood to mean.
 
### Step 5d\. The overlap period (AD 30-70)
 
A related historical clarification, since apostolic temple attendance is sometimes offered as a precedent for retaining Old Testament ceremonial: between the cross and the fall of Jerusalem in AD 70 there was a roughly forty-year overlap in which the shadow still stood while the substance reigned. Jewish Christians continued to attend the temple at the hours of prayer (Acts 3:1) and taught in its courts (5:42), and Paul once joined a vow completion entailing offerings, at James's urging, expressly to show he did not teach Jews to abandon Moses (Acts 21:23-26). Hebrews, apparently written while the cult still operated, describes the entire apparatus as obsolete and "ready to vanish away" (8:13). For Gentiles the apostolic ruling ran the other way: the Jerusalem council declined to circumcise them (Acts 15), which by that decision barred them from Passover and full cultic participation (Exod 12:48). So conduct tolerated during the overlap is accommodation to a passing order, not warrant for perpetuating it, and the one moment the apostolic church could have brought the incoming nations to the altar, it deliberately did not.
 
### Step 6\. The Passover analogy
 
"Christ our passover is sacrificed for us: therefore let us keep the feast" (1 Cor 5:7-8). Paul names the fulfillment and then commands the church to keep the feast, with "the unleavened bread of sincerity and truth," not by slaughtering a lamb. The command lands on the signified reality, not on the continuation of the sign.
 
Incense follows the same pattern: Revelation 5:8 names the type's meaning (the prayers of the saints), and 1 Timothy 2:8 commands the offering of exactly that, in every place. We keep the feast without the lamb; we offer the prayers without the smoke.
 
A possible objection is that "keep the feast" refers to the Lord's Supper, which does have physical elements. This actually strengthens rather than weakens the analogy: insofar as any table observance continues, Christ personally instituted the new form, with his own words, specified elements, and apostolic confirmation. That is what continuation of a type's physical sign looks like when God intends it to continue: it receives a new institution. The Supper's physical elements are not a counterexample to the argument; they are proof that continuation requires institution. Incense has no such institution.
 
A secondary analogy: circumcision. Colossians 2:11-12 names the reality (the circumcision made without hands), and Galatians 5:2 treats re-enacting the sign as regression, not enrichment. The Passover analogy is the stronger of the two because it includes a positive command to offer the signified reality.
 
### Step 7\. The heaven's liturgy objection
 
The strongest pro-incense argument from Revelation is not simply that Revelation depicts incense, but that the church's earthly worship participates in and mirrors the heavenly worship, and since the heavenly worship includes incense (Rev 5:8; 8:3-5), the earthly worship should as well.
 
The response is to affirm the participation and then limit the inference:
 
The New Testament does teach that gathered worship joins the assembly of heaven (Heb 12:22-24), and this is a rich and true point. The question is not whether we join heaven's worship but whether a vision of symbolic furniture prescribes its physical reproduction on earth.
 
The vision interprets its own symbols. The robes in Revelation are the righteousness of the saints (Rev 7:14; 19:8). The incense is the prayers of the saints (Rev 5:8). The same interpretive move governs both; one hermeneutic must govern the throne room. Nobody argues that the church should install a sea of glass, 24 thrones, or golden harps because these appear in the vision. The consistent hermeneutic is that the throne room imagery describes the realities of which the church's worship consists, not the furnishings it must reproduce.
 
Furthermore, if the incense in the vision is the prayers of the saints (which the verse states), then the vision is a depiction of the very thing the church offers when it prays. Reproducing the symbol alongside the reality it signifies is not enrichment; it points backward from a fulfillment already possessed.
 
### Step 8\. For the interlocutor who rejects the regulative principle
 
If someone says the regulative principle is too strict and that permission (rather than positive warrant) suffices for a worship practice, the argument against incense does not depend on the regulative principle:
 
1. Even the normative principle forbids what Scripture forbids, not merely what Scripture commands against. Reviving a fulfilled shadow is the specific thing Colossians 2:16-17 and Galatians 4:9-10 warn against. The Colossians passage tells the church not to let anyone bind them with the shadows now that the body has come; Galatians names returning to the weak and beggarly elements as the error itself. These are not arguments from silence but positive warnings about a defined category of act.  
     
2. Permission and the actual defense of incense are in tension. If incense stands by mere permission, it is a thing indifferent, a human ceremony, optional and changeable. Historically, the Anglican tradition would call this an Article 34 matter: ceremonies ordained by human authority that the church may appoint or change. But the defenders of incense do not treat it as indifferent. They defend it as a Godward offering, the fulfillment of Malachi, a reflection of heaven's liturgy. A Godward offering cannot rest on bare permission; worship offered to God must be acceptable to God (Heb 12:28), and self-devised worship is what Colossians 2:23 calls will-worship. So the fork: defend incense like flowers and drop the Malachi warrant and the heavenly-liturgy theology, or keep those warrants and accept that permission is not enough even on generous principles.
### Step 9\. The Anglican-history note (supporting context)
 
This is not an argument from antiquity (older does not mean truer), but it is a useful correction to the narrative that the Reformed tradition is the innovator and the Anglo-Catholic tradition is the received historic practice.
 
English church worship was plain and essentially incense-free from the Edwardian Reformation (late 1540s) until the ritualist revival of the 1850s onward, a development associated with the second generation of the Oxford Movement. When the ceremonial use of incense was legally tested, it was condemned: the Court of Arches ruled against it in Elphinstone v. Purchas (1870), and the 1899 Lambeth Opinion (Archbishops Temple of Canterbury and Maclagan of York) stated that the use of incense in public worship "is not at present enjoined nor permitted by the law of the Church of England," with clergy requested to discontinue it. The Opinion further specified that if used at all, incense must be used "to sweeten the Church, and outside the worship altogether," drawing precisely the element/circumstance line described in Step 2 above. The Church of Ireland's Book of Common Prayer contained an outright ban: "No incense or any substitution therefore or imitation thereof shall at any time be used in any church or chapel or other place in which the public services of the Church are celebrated."
 
The 1899 Opinion has no legal force today: the Worship and Doctrine Measure 1974 repealed the Act of Uniformity 1662, dissolving the enforcement regime, and practices not in the Prayer Book became prima facie permitted rather than forbidden. But nothing ever reversed the Opinion's reasoning or positively authorized incense; the sequence is condemned when squarely tested, practiced in defiance of the ruling, and the prohibition machinery eventually dismantled around the continuing practice. This is legalization by attrition, not authorization. The point is simply that the practice at issue descends from a nineteenth-century ritualist revival, not from the historic Anglican formularies. For three centuries the church that produced those formularies worshiped without a censer.
 
A common counterexample is Lancelot Andrewes, who burned incense in his private chapel. His case concedes the point: the exceptions were private, party-marked, and unauthorized by the prayer book. The public liturgy from Cranmer to the ritualists knew no censer.
 
### Step 10\. The tradition diagnosis (reserve)
 
None of the texts cited as warrant specifies a thurible, censing points, censing personnel, or a compound. Every concrete feature of the practice comes from post-apostolic ecclesiastical tradition. This is a recognizable pattern in high-church Anglican and Catholic apologetics generally: a practice is maintained on the basis of received tradition, Scripture texts are then recruited to show that tradition is not forbidden (or is even encouraged), and the texts are cited as though they were the original ground. The honest question this pattern raises is whether tradition is the actual warrant, and if so, whether that case should be made openly on its own terms rather than through Scripture texts that do not specify what is being practiced.
